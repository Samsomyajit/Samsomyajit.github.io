# Feminism, Men, and the Discipline of a Soft Heart: A Personal Reflection

## Author's note

I am writing this piece as a personal reflection in my own voice. I do not see feminism as a slogan, a trend, or a simple contest between men and women. I see it as a serious moral and social question about dignity, justice, power, and the kind of society we want to build. At the same time, I also want to reflect on something more intimate: what it means for a man like me, someone with a soft heart, to relate to women with sincerity, care, and emotional seriousness. In particular, I want to connect this reflection to my own experience with my wife, Yanrong, because marriage has made these questions far more real to me than abstract debate ever could.

## What feminism means to me

In the broadest sense, feminism is the belief that women deserve full political, social, economic, and personal equality, and that societies must confront the structures that deny them that equality (Britannica, 2026). That basic definition matters because too many discussions about feminism begin from caricature rather than clarity. Some people speak about feminism as if it is merely anger toward men; others treat it as a fashionable label that can be used without responsibility. I do not agree with either view. To me, feminism is best understood as a serious ethical and political effort to correct deeply rooted inequalities.

Feminist thought also became richer over time. It was never only about one demand or one slogan. Feminist philosophy expanded the discussion from rights alone to larger issues of power, embodiment, care, work, sexuality, family, and the everyday ways in which social norms shape moral life (McAfee, 2018; Norlock, 2019). This is why feminism cannot be reduced to a single textbook definition. It is both a movement and a way of questioning the social order.

Historically, people often describe feminism in "waves," even though that language is imperfect. The first wave focused strongly on legal rights and suffrage; the second widened the discussion to social, legal, familial, and workplace inequalities; and later feminist thought became more attentive to diversity, identity, sexuality, race, and the limits of earlier feminist frameworks (Britannica, 2026). I think the value of this historical view is not just classification. It reminds us that feminism developed because older social arrangements repeatedly failed women, and because each generation had to identify new forms of inequality that earlier generations had not fully addressed.

I also want to correct a mistake that is often casually repeated, and which I myself once echoed in rough form: if one wants to look for a classical philosophical antecedent for discussions of women's capacities, the relevant Platonic text is *The Republic*, not *Atlantis*. In *The Republic*, Plato entertains the idea that women in the guardian class can receive similar education and perform similar political roles, even if his framework remains far from modern feminism (Brown, 2003). The point here is not to claim that feminism began in ancient Greece, because it did not in the modern sense. The point is simply that questions about women, justice, and public life have long philosophical roots.

## Why feminism still matters

Feminism still matters because inequality has not disappeared; it has merely changed form. In many places women have gained legal recognition while continuing to bear unequal burdens in domestic labor, care work, workplace advancement, public safety, and social expectation. Feminist ethics is valuable precisely because it asks us to examine not only the law, but also the moral habits and assumptions through which privilege is normalized and suffering is made invisible (Norlock, 2019).

For me, one of the strongest things feminism does is force us to notice what society often takes for granted. It asks uncomfortable questions. Who gets heard? Who is expected to compromise? Who performs emotional labor without recognition? Who is taught to shrink, absorb, adjust, and endure? These are not small matters. They shape daily life. They shape ambition, self-worth, freedom, and even the private texture of love.

At the same time, I do not think every statement made in the name of feminism is automatically wise. Like any major movement, feminism can be simplified, distorted, commercialized, or used performatively. Some people use the language of justice without the discipline of justice. Some turn moral seriousness into self-righteousness. But the misuse of a principle is not an argument against the principle itself. The answer to distortion is not abandonment; it is clarification. If feminism is a struggle for dignity and fairness, then it must remain accountable to those very values.

## A discourse on feminism in men

The part that matters most to me is this: what does feminism look like in men?

I do not think feminism in men means speaking over women, performing moral superiority, or using progressive language for self-image. Nor do I think it means abandoning masculinity and pretending that manhood itself is the problem. To me, feminism in men means learning how to live without entitlement. It means refusing to build one's identity on domination, emotional indifference, or the assumption that care belongs to women while authority belongs to men.

This is where I believe many men struggle. Boys are often taught, directly or indirectly, that hardness is maturity, that emotional restraint is strength, and that tenderness is weakness. Yet that model of masculinity is often profoundly impoverished. It may produce pride, but not depth; control, but not understanding. UN Women has repeatedly argued that men and boys are essential to advancing gender equality because they can challenge sexist norms, confront misogyny, and help change institutions as well as everyday behaviour (UN Women, 2021; UN Women, 2024). I think this is true, but it must begin inwardly. A man cannot meaningfully support women's dignity while still privately clinging to the comforts of male entitlement.

For that reason, I think feminism in men is not just a political position. It is a moral discipline. It requires listening without defensiveness, caring without patronizing, and learning to recognize forms of harm that one may never personally experience. It asks a man to see women not as symbols, rewards, obligations, or threats, but as full persons whose inner lives matter as much as his own.

## On having a soft heart as a man

I have often felt that one of my defining qualities is that I have a soft heart. For a long time, I was not sure whether to see that as a strength or a vulnerability. The world often rewards men for certainty, toughness, emotional distance, and strategic coldness. In such an environment, softness can feel dangerous. It can make a man worry that he will be judged as weak, overly emotional, or insufficiently masculine.

But I no longer think that way. I now see softness, at least in its healthier form, as a moral capacity. A soft heart is not the same as lack of courage. It is not passivity, and it is not sentimental confusion. Rather, it is the ability to remain affected by the reality of other people. It is the refusal to let strength harden into cruelty.

Research on empathy complicates many popular stereotypes. Reviews of the literature suggest that average sex differences in empathy do exist, but they are shaped by a complex interaction of biology, culture, development, and social expectation; empathy should therefore not be treated as some mysterious quality belonging only to women and denied to men (Christov-Moore *et al*., 2014). That matters to me, because I do not want to describe my softness as something unmanly. I want to describe it as something fully human. If anything, a humane masculinity should be capable of feeling deeply without collapsing into insecurity.

When a man has a soft heart, he can notice what harsher models of masculinity often miss. He can notice exhaustion behind a composed face. He can notice the pressure of unspoken expectation. He can notice the emotional labour that women are often expected to perform quietly. He can also notice how easy it is for men to speak of women in abstract terms while remaining ignorant of the ordinary burdens women carry in relationships, families, workplaces, and public life. A soft heart does not solve inequality by itself, but it can make a man more receptive to truth.

## What my experience with Yanrong has taught me

My experience with my wife, Yanrong, has made these reflections much more concrete. Marriage has a way of exposing whether one's values are real or merely decorative. It is easy to speak in grand ideas about fairness, care, and respect; it is much harder to live them patiently in daily life.

Being married has made me think more seriously about partnership, security, responsibility, and the future. It has made me more attentive to the fact that love is not possession; it is stewardship, presence, and mutual recognition. When I think about Yanrong, I do not simply think of "women" as a category. I think of a real human being with her own interior world, pressures, strengths, fears, dignity, hopes, and emotional burdens. That shift matters. It moves my understanding of feminism away from abstract rhetoric and into lived ethics.

Through married life, I have become more sensitive to the quiet forms of labour that are easy to overlook. Not all labour is financial or visibly measurable. Some of it is emotional steadiness, relational memory, silent accommodation, worry, patience, and the daily effort of holding life together without making a spectacle of it. Loving my wife has made me reflect more deeply on how often women are expected to carry these burdens without recognition. It has also made me ask harder questions of myself: am I truly listening, or only reacting? Am I creating space, or unconsciously taking it? Am I caring in ways that lighten another person's load, or only in ways that make me feel virtuous?

My own softness has helped me here. Because I feel deeply, I can more easily be moved by what my wife experiences, even when I do not experience it in the same way. That does not mean I always understand perfectly. No husband can assume perfect understanding. But it does mean I am willing to stay present, to care, and to let another person's reality challenge my own habits. In that sense, my soft heart has not made me less of a man. It has made me more responsible in how I try to love.

There is also another dimension to this. Thinking about work, stability, money, and the future of a family has made me feel the weight of responsibility more sharply. Yet rather than pushing me toward a colder version of masculinity, that pressure has made me value care even more. I want to support my family not only materially, but also emotionally and ethically. I do not want to become a man who is outwardly dependable but inwardly hardened. I want my strength to remain humane.

## Feminism, balance, and moral seriousness

I therefore do not see feminism and manhood as opposites. I see feminism, at its best, as a challenge to defective models of manhood. It asks men to become better, not smaller. It asks us to exchange entitlement for responsibility, distance for attentiveness, and ego for moral seriousness. That does not mean men and women are identical in every respect, nor does it require the erasure of difference. What it does require is equal dignity, equal seriousness, and equal moral worth.

I also think feminism should remain intellectually honest. It should not become a new dogma in which all complexity is forbidden. A movement that exists to expose injustice must also guard against becoming unjust in its own habits of thought. There must be room for criticism, self-correction, and nuance. But that nuance should never be used as an excuse to dismiss the fundamental necessity of the movement. If women continue to face unequal burdens, then feminism remains necessary.

For me personally, the most meaningful form of feminism is not performative, combative, or fashionable. It is ethical. It is visible in how one listens, how one speaks, how one shares power, how one interprets labour, and how one treats the vulnerability of another human being. It is also visible in marriage, because marriage reveals whether one truly believes in partnership or merely enjoys being served by it.

## Conclusion

My final view is simple. I believe feminism is necessary because justice between men and women is still incomplete. I also believe that men have an important place within that struggle, not as owners of the discourse, but as participants in moral change. For me, having a soft heart is not a weakness that stands against masculinity. It is one of the reasons I can care about women sincerely, relate to their vulnerability more seriously, and resist the kind of masculinity that confuses hardness with worth.

My life with Yanrong has made this belief more personal and more demanding. Through love, companionship, and responsibility, I have come to see that caring about women is not a sentimental extra; it is part of becoming a decent human being. If my softness helps me listen better, care more deeply, and love more responsibly, then I do not want to lose it. I want to refine it into strength.
