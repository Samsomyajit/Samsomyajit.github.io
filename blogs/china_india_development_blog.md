# Why China Pulled Ahead While India Moved More Slowly: Work, State Capacity, and the Politics of Development

> “But if a few awake, you can’t say there is no hope of destroying the iron house.”  
> — Lu Xun, *Preface to Call to Arms* (Lu Xun, 1922)

## Introduction: the real comparison is not talent, but organisation

A familiar question appears again and again in conversations about Asia’s rise: if India also has brilliant people, old centres of learning, engineers, doctors, entrepreneurs, and a huge population, why did China industrialise and transform itself so much faster?

The answer is not that Indians are less talented, less hardworking, or less intelligent. It is also not simply that China had a bigger GDP. The deeper answer is that China, especially after 1978, built a development system that was able to absorb, direct, and discipline the labour of enormous numbers of ordinary people across agriculture, factories, construction, logistics, local administration, and export manufacturing. It turned millions of farmers into industrial workers, millions of villages into production nodes, and many coastal cities into engines of world trade (World Bank and DRC, 2022; Zeng, 2011; OECD, 2009).

This is where the contrast with India becomes sharp. India has achieved major successes, and it remains one of the fastest-growing major economies in the world even in 2026 (World Bank, 2026a). But India’s structural transformation has been slower and more uneven. Agriculture still accounts for a very large share of employment, while the economy has not generated enough high-quality jobs in manufacturing and modern services to match the aspirations of its young population (World Bank, 2026b; IMF, 2024). The result is a painful contradiction: educational expansion without enough employment expansion.

To understand this divergence, one must look at several layers at once: ideology, state capacity, geography, labour mobilisation, urbanisation, export strategy, leadership quality, and social values about work. One must also understand the cultural psychology behind modern Chinese reform and the social psychology behind India’s employment crisis.

In that sense, Lu Xun and Yu Dafu are not ornamental references. They are part of the story. Lu Xun stands for the moral necessity of awakening a society trapped inside an “iron house” of old habits and decaying institutions (Lu Xun, 1922; Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026a). Yu Dafu, who began with intensely personal anguish and later shifted toward concern for “the state of the masses,” captures another emotional truth of modern China: national humiliation was transformed into a developmental will (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026b). That emotional conversion mattered. China’s twentieth century did not only produce revolution. It produced a state determined not to remain weak.

## 1. China’s rise was built on broad participation, not just elite brilliance

One of the most important findings from development research on China is that poverty reduction and economic growth were not driven by one policy alone. China’s success came from layered and cumulative change.

The World Bank and the Development Research Center of the State Council argue that China’s growth was tied to **broad-based economic transformation**. Reform began in agriculture, where market incentives raised productivity. Low-skilled, labour-intensive industries then created employment for workers released from farming. Urbanisation allowed migrants to access city jobs, and infrastructure connected rural areas to urban and export markets (World Bank and DRC, 2022). This is crucial. Development did not stay trapped in a few elite enclaves. It spread through the participation of huge numbers of ordinary workers.

That pattern helps explain why China reduced poverty on a historically unprecedented scale. According to the World Bank and DRC, the number of people living below the international poverty line fell by close to **800 million** over four decades, accounting for roughly three-quarters of global extreme-poverty reduction over that period (World Bank and DRC, 2022). That scale is difficult to understand unless we see China’s rise as a mass labour transformation.

The early rural reforms were especially important. The **household responsibility system** sharply increased incentives in agriculture; by the end of 1983, around 94.2 per cent of rural households were under the new system, and research cited by the World Bank suggests that the shift accounted for 30 to 50 per cent of the rise in agricultural output between 1978 and 1984 (World Bank and DRC, 2022). This mattered because it did two things at once: it raised rural incomes and released labour for non-farm activity.

Then came the rise of **township and village enterprises (TVEs)**. These enterprises absorbed surplus rural labour and became one of the most important bridges between farm life and industrial work. The World Bank reports that between 1978 and 1996 TVE employment surged to more than 130 million jobs, while the number of TVEs rose from 1.5 million to 23 million, most in light industry (World Bank and DRC, 2022). This is the kind of mechanism India never developed at the same scale. China’s development path gave ordinary villagers a route into production. India’s has often left too many people either stuck in low-productivity agriculture or waiting for a narrow set of urban white-collar opportunities.

So when people say China grew because of “cheap labour,” they are only partly right. Labour was cheap, but many countries have had cheap labour. China’s real achievement was organisational: it created institutions that could move labour from one productivity regime to another.

## 2. “Chinese-flavoured communism”: not doctrinal purity, but disciplined pragmatism

It is more accurate to say that China succeeded not through pure command socialism and not through laissez-faire capitalism, but through a highly pragmatic system often described as **socialism with Chinese characteristics**. Its strength lay in selective marketisation inside a politically centralised state.

Deng Xiaoping’s reform era did not abandon state power. It redirected it. The post-1978 leadership made the **Four Modernizations**—agriculture, industry, science and technology, and defence—the basis for a new national priority: economic development rather than ideological mobilisation for its own sake (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026c). Britannica notes that this shift marked a significant departure from the preceding era and became the basis of the policies behind China’s rapid growth in the 1980s and 1990s (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026c).

China also turned decisively toward the international economy after 1978. The leadership sought foreign capital, trade, technology, and managerial know-how. Special economic zones (SEZs) were introduced along the southern coast, then opening widened to more cities and regions (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026d). These zones did not simply attract investment; they served as laboratories for new institutions. Zeng (2011) argues that SEZs and industrial clusters were two of the main engines of China’s development, contributing significantly to GDP, employment, exports, technology transfer, and foreign investment. In a World Bank summary of China’s SEZ experience, such zones are credited with 22 per cent of GDP, 45 per cent of FDI, 60 per cent of exports, and over 30 million jobs (World Bank, 2015).

The deeper lesson is that Chinese communism was successful not when it tried to control everything directly, but when it retained political control while allowing disciplined economic experimentation. Local pilots could be tested and scaled up nationally. The World Bank and DRC explicitly identify the household responsibility system, TVE reform, and special economic zones as examples of successful local pilots later expanded across the country (World Bank and DRC, 2022).

This model had obvious costs and limits. It was never politically liberal. It relied on strong party control, hierarchical discipline, and restricted dissent. Contemporary China also faces serious headwinds: property-sector weakness, cautious household consumption, high precautionary savings, uneven social protection, and pressure on private investment and the labour market (World Bank, 2025). So the argument here is not that China created a perfect model. It is that, for several decades, it created a **high-capacity developmental state** that was unusually effective at coordinating labour, capital, exports, infrastructure, and local implementation.

That is why “Chinese-flavoured communism” matters historically. It was not successful because it was communist in a textbook sense. It was successful because it fused political control with economic pragmatism and long-horizon statecraft.

## 3. Leadership mattered: Deng, Jiang, and Hu were not accidental figures

A country’s institutions matter more than individual hero worship. But leadership quality still matters, especially during periods of strategic transition. In China’s case, the post-Mao reform period was shaped by a strikingly educated and technocratic leadership cohort.

**Deng Xiaoping** studied in France and the Soviet Union before returning to China, and he later became the paramount figure of the reform era (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026e). What made Deng historically important was not only personal intelligence but strategic clarity. He understood that post-Cultural-Revolution China could not remain trapped in ideological rigidity. He pushed the country toward modernisation, foreign engagement, and policy experimentation, while keeping the political dominance of the Communist Party intact (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026c; Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026d; Vogel, 2011).

**Jiang Zemin** graduated from Shanghai Jiao Tong University with a degree in electrical engineering, worked as an engineer, and received additional technical training in the Soviet Union (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026f). Britannica describes him as combining continued market reform with insistence on the CCP’s monopoly of political power (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026f). Under Jiang, China accelerated integration with the world economy, deepened market reforms, and consolidated the post-Deng reform trajectory.

**Hu Jintao** earned a hydroelectric engineering degree from Tsinghua University and rose through both technical and political roles (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026g). Britannica notes that even in provincial leadership he was associated with educational and economic reforms (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026g). Hu’s period is often remembered as less charismatic than Deng’s, but it continued the logic of technocratic governance, infrastructure-led expansion, and administrative scale.

The point is not that engineering degrees automatically produce good leaders. The point is that China’s post-1978 elite often had technical training, developmental focus, and long planning horizons. Reform-era China was shaped by leaders who thought in terms of systems: agriculture, power, industry, logistics, trade, technology, and administrative implementation.

Compared with this, India has certainly had capable leaders and extraordinary thinkers. But its leadership pipeline has often been less consistently developmental in orientation and more constrained by electoral turnover, coalition bargaining, patronage pressures, bureaucratic fragmentation, and state-level variation. The problem is not simply that individuals were “weak.” The problem is that India has struggled to produce the same degree of sustained policy continuity around mass industrial transformation.

That distinction matters. A developmental state does not emerge merely from intelligence. It emerges from the marriage of elite competence, institutional discipline, and implementation capacity.

## 4. Geography helped China, but geography alone did not explain China

Geography is not destiny, but it changes what is possible.

China’s long coastline, major river systems, and proximity to East Asian production networks gave it enormous advantages once reform began. When China opened itself to trade and investment, coastal cities became export platforms, manufacturing hubs, and urban magnets. Britannica notes that opening sections of the country to foreign trade and investment, especially through coastal SEZs, had remarkable success, with Shenzhen becoming the most famous case (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026d). OECD analysis adds that China’s metropolitan regions became central to improving productivity through ever-widening supply chains, and that the transformation of Chinese cities into “the world’s factory” changed global flows of resources, products, capital, technology, information, and people (OECD, 2009).

But geography only became economically decisive because policy activated it. Ports matter only if roads, customs, industrial zones, electricity, labour supply, and export firms are aligned. China did that alignment better than almost any developing country in modern history.

India also has a large coastline and several major urban centres. Its problem has not been pure geographical disadvantage. Rather, it has been weaker integration of geography with industrial policy, logistics, urban governance, and labour-intensive manufacturing strategy. This is why India grew strongly in some services and technology sectors without generating a China-like manufacturing takeoff.

So geography explains part of the story—but only in interaction with state capacity.

## 5. India’s problem is not lack of talent, but incomplete structural transformation

India is not a failed economy. It is a large, increasingly important economy with major strengths in services, information technology, pharmaceuticals, and entrepreneurship. As of April 2026, the World Bank still describes India as among the fastest-growing major economies (World Bank, 2026a). Yet growth alone does not settle the comparison with China.

The more important question is whether growth has changed the **employment structure** fast enough.

The IMF’s 2024 working paper on India’s structural transformation argues that India’s transition remains incomplete and estimates that the country needs to create between **143 million and 324 million jobs by 2050**. It also argues that shifting workers toward more dynamic sectors could raise GDP growth further (IMF, 2024). The World Bank’s 2026 summary of the India Country Economic Memorandum makes the challenge even more concrete: **around 45 per cent of employment remains in agriculture**, and stronger allocation of land, labour, and capital toward manufacturing and services is needed to raise productivity (World Bank, 2026b).

That single contrast already says a lot. China’s growth was deeply tied to moving labour out of agriculture into factories, construction, logistics, and urban production networks. India’s growth has often been described as more service-led and less employment-intensive. In practical terms, that means many workers remain trapped in low-productivity activity even while the aggregate economy expands.

This is why educated unemployment in India has become so politically and socially explosive. The India Employment Report 2024, produced jointly by the ILO and the Institute for Human Development, shows that youth unemployment is disproportionately concentrated among the educated. In 2022, youth unemployment for those with secondary education or higher was **18.4 per cent**, and for graduates it was **29.1 per cent**, compared with only **3.4 per cent** for those who could not read or write (ILO and IHD, 2024). The same report states that **82.9 per cent of all unemployed people in 2022 were youth**, and that unemployment in India had become predominantly a problem among the educated young (ILO and IHD, 2024).

This is not merely a labour-market statistic. It is a social diagnosis. It means India has expanded aspirations faster than it has expanded suitable work.

## 6. Prestige, respectability, and the crowding of aspiration

Here the comparison becomes cultural as well as economic.

In India, a narrow band of professions—medicine, engineering, banking, civil service, teaching, and government employment—often carries disproportionate social prestige. Families frequently do not judge work only by income; they judge it by status, stability, and marriage value. In moderation this is understandable. In excess it becomes economically destructive.

The India Employment Report 2024 captures this clearly. It contains a section titled **“Too many educated youths rushing for few government jobs”** and notes that Indian media frequently report cases of thousands of educated young people applying for a small number of public vacancies, often far below their qualifications (ILO and IHD, 2024). The report also finds that more than one quarter of surveyed students planned to seek a government job, and that youth aspirations were “largely white-collar public sector jobs,” driven by desires for high income and job stability (ILO and IHD, 2024).

This matters because prestige can distort labour allocation. If too many talented people are steered toward a narrow set of socially approved occupations, then other sectors—manufacturing management, industrial design, vocational craftsmanship, logistics, food processing, machine maintenance, local entrepreneurship, export trading, construction technology, municipal systems, and skilled technical services—remain undervalued. The economy then produces a strange imbalance: intense competition for a few respectable jobs and too little respect for the broader ecology of productive work.

China’s rise was different in this respect. The reform period did not erase hierarchy or status, but it normalised the idea that millions could move through factories, workshops, construction sites, local administrations, and export firms without such work being treated as socially shameful. The political culture of revolution, mass mobilisation, and socialist production had already weakened some older prejudices against manual and collective labour. Reform-era marketisation then gave those forms of labour higher returns.

India, by contrast, still struggles with a cultural economy in which “success” is often imagined vertically rather than widely. Too many people are taught to climb into a tiny tower instead of helping build a broad productive base.

## 7. The real leadership problem in India is state capacity, not just personalities

It is tempting to reduce India’s slower development to “bad leaders” or “unskilled leaders.” That language may feel emotionally satisfying, but it is analytically weak.

The more serious problem is that India’s political system often converts public energy into fragmented bargaining rather than long-horizon developmental execution. Bardhan (2010) argues that democratic governance in India has often been marred by accountability failures, while Ganguly and Mukherji (2012) describe a political landscape transformed by competing mobilisations and institutional shifts rather than single-minded developmental coordination. India’s democratic pluralism is valuable in many moral and political respects, but it also complicates coherent industrial transformation.

That does not mean democracy causes underdevelopment. It means democracy without strong state capacity, administrative discipline, and implementation coherence can produce uneven results.

China, especially from Deng through Hu, benefited from:
- stronger central signalling;
- a bureaucratic career system that rewarded implementation;
- willingness to use local pilots and then scale them;
- emphasis on infrastructure and export competitiveness;
- political ability to subordinate many short-term conflicts to long-term industrial goals.

India has often been weaker on exactly these margins:
- compliance burdens remain heavy (World Bank, 2026b);
- labour and land reallocation remain difficult (World Bank, 2026b);
- productive sectors do not expand quickly enough to absorb labour (IMF, 2024);
- public aspiration is crowded into a narrow set of jobs (ILO and IHD, 2024).

So the issue is not that Indians lack brilliance. It is that brilliance is too often stranded in islands while the state fails to build the bridges.

## 8. Lu Xun, Yu Dafu, and the psychology of national transformation

Why bring literature into a discussion of economics?

Because development is also psychological. It depends on what a society feels about weakness, dignity, shame, and effort.

Lu Xun became the great critic of China’s inherited stagnation and moral numbness. His “iron house” image remains one of the clearest metaphors for a civilisation trapped inside suffocating habits while still asleep (Lu Xun, 1922; Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026a). In developmental terms, Lu Xun represents intellectual awakening: the refusal to accept decay as normal.

Yu Dafu represents something complementary. Britannica notes that he was one of the founding members of the Creation Society, studied in Japan, and later shifted from self-preoccupation toward concern with the masses (Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2026b). That shift is symbolically important. The wounded self becomes the national self; private humiliation becomes collective urgency.

Modern China’s development cannot be explained by literary mood alone. But the emotional texture matters. A country that has deeply internalised humiliation may become intensely serious about power, industry, and sovereignty. China’s twentieth-century memory of weakness helped make economic strength a civilisational mission.

India also has civilisational self-confidence, but its social imagination of development has often been different. It is rich in individual excellence and moral discourse, yet less consistent in cultivating broad respect for productive labour across the whole social pyramid. That difference affects how societies reward effort.

Lu Xun’s challenge still applies: waking people up is painful, but without awakening there is no escape from the iron house.

## 9. What India can learn from China without copying China mechanically

India does not need to become China. It cannot become China, because its political structure, social diversity, federal design, and historical path are different. But it can still learn from China’s experience in several concrete ways.

### First, restore dignity to all productive work
Industrial labour, vocational skill, manufacturing management, logistics, maintenance, machine work, and technical trades must become socially respectable. A country cannot become prosperous if too many citizens are taught to look down on the work that actually builds prosperity.

### Second, make structural transformation an employment mission
The central challenge is not only GDP growth. It is shifting workers from low-productivity agriculture into higher-productivity activity. That means labour-intensive manufacturing, supply chains, urban industrial services, and export platforms.

### Third, narrow the aspiration-job mismatch
If white-collar public sector jobs remain the dream of a large share of educated youth, India will continue to waste energy in examination bottlenecks and credential inflation (ILO and IHD, 2024).

### Fourth, build technocratic capacity without abandoning democracy
India needs more competent long-horizon administration, not less democracy. Policy continuity, infrastructure execution, municipal capacity, and regulatory predictability matter as much as elections.

### Fifth, think geographically
Coastal manufacturing corridors, logistics reform, industrial clusters, and city-region governance can convert geography into productivity. China’s experience with SEZs and metropolitan growth shows what coordinated spatial strategy can achieve (Zeng, 2011; OECD, 2009).

### Sixth, treat education as linked to production, not prestige
A degree should not be a symbolic badge detached from the economy’s real needs. Education policy must be integrated with industrial policy, skills policy, and urban job creation.

---

## Conclusion: development is a moral and organisational achievement

China’s rise was not a miracle of race, culture, or obedience. It was the outcome of a hard political project: reform agriculture, absorb surplus labour, build rural industry, urbanise at scale, connect infrastructure to exports, use pilot reforms, attract capital and technology, and maintain state coherence over decades. Its version of communism succeeded where it became pragmatic, developmental, and organisationally effective.

India’s slower pace is not proof of civilisational weakness. It is proof that talent without broad labour mobilisation, high social respect for all forms of work, and strong state capacity does not automatically produce national transformation.

The tragedy in India is not that there are too few brilliant people. The tragedy is that too many brilliant people are trained to chase too few socially approved destinations while the wider economy remains under-organised. The nation then produces not only unemployment, but humiliation.

China’s lesson, therefore, is not simply “be authoritarian” or “copy communism.” The real lesson is harsher and more practical: a society develops when it honours production, channels aspiration into many sectors, and creates leaders and institutions capable of turning national intelligence into coordinated work.

Lu Xun warned that societies can suffocate while asleep. China, for a time, found a way to awaken and organise itself. India’s challenge is not to admire that from a distance, but to ask whether it is prepared to awaken in its own way.
