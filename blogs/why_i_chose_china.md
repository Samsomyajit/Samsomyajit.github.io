> **A note before the journey begins.** This is not a political manifesto. It is a personal chapter: a story about inheritance, loneliness, books, friendship, love, migration, scholarship, and the strange feeling of belonging to several civilizations at once.

![My Trinity Gentleman self — formal, theatrical, perhaps a little too fond of old-world manners.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/01-trinity-gentleman-self.jpg)

*My “Trinity Gentleman” self: the version of me that people sometimes mistake for snobbery, when it is really a costume stitched from literature, loneliness, and admiration for a certain idea of gentleness.*

---

## Chapter I — The Western Door

Back in India, many people still carry a quiet assumption: to go to Europe or America is to have arrived.

It is not always spoken crudely. It comes dressed as advice, aspiration, family pride, and social arithmetic. A son in America, a daughter in Europe, a cousin in London, a friend in Dublin, a classmate in California — these become signs of progress, almost like medals pinned invisibly to the family name.

I understand why this happens. The West has universities, libraries, laboratories, old cities, legal systems, museums, and a long tradition of intellectual life. One should not deny what is admirable. But there is also something else beneath the admiration: a colonial aftertaste, a habit of looking outward for validation, a belief that one becomes more real only when certified by a Western geography.

That is the part I have always struggled with.

The tragedy of colonial dogmatism is not only that it dominates the colonized from outside. It survives inside the imagination. It teaches people to rank themselves by proximity to someone else’s centre. It whispers that dignity is elsewhere, that intelligence must be stamped by Europe, that refinement comes from imitation, that success means escaping the place from which one began.

And yet, strangely, I too was shaped by that world.

I loved English literature. I loved the sound of old drawing-room wit, Anglican mysteries, academic eccentricity, Oxbridge manners, Victorian shadows, Edwardian comedy, and the peculiar moral universe in which a sentence could be both polite and lethal. I read P. G. Wodehouse and laughed at Jeeves and Wooster; I adored the performances of Stephen Fry and Hugh Laurie; I watched Sherlock Holmes, Father Brown, Blackadder, and Yes Minister until their cadences became part of my inner weather. In my head, Mr. Hacker and Sir Humphrey Appleby were not merely comic characters. They were tutors in rhetoric, evasion, ceremony, and civilization’s absurdity.

I argued about Shakespeare and Marlowe with my English teacher. I drifted from the Brontë sisters to detective fiction, from drawing-room comedy to metaphysical questions, from old English manners to the mathematics of Euler, Ramanujan, Hardy, Littlewood, Einstein, Schrödinger, Penrose, Thorne, Jacobi, and Hawking.

That mixture made me strange.

I did not quite fit the ordinary categories around me. I was not trying to look down on anyone. But I liked careful speech, polished dress, long conversations, old books, private codes of dignity, and a certain theatrical gentleness. I have sometimes called myself aristocratic, not because I believe I am superior, but because I grew up with a family memory of lineage, refinement, and ancestry. In my own private mythology, even the story of roots reaching back toward Buddha became less a claim of superiority than a reminder that inheritance is a responsibility.

To be aristocratic, at its best, is not to sneer.

It is to practice restraint.

It is to value learning.

It is to know that manners without kindness are empty performance.

But outside, people do not always read the soul correctly. Sometimes what is gentleness appears as distance. Sometimes what is shyness appears as pride. Sometimes what is loneliness appears as disdain.

I became an outcast in my own country, not because I hated it, but because I did not know how to inhabit it simply.

I was Indian, but not only Indian. I was drawn to Europe, but not European. I was modern, but haunted by old things. I loved science, but also myth. I wrote code, but wanted books. I worked in industry, but felt my spirit drying inside the machinery of corporate life.

That is where the journey truly began.

## Chapter II — The Industry Years and the Desire to Escape

Before Ireland, I worked as a software engineer.

Many people would call that success. It had a salary, a title, a stable path. But inside, I felt something being slowly crushed.

The work was not evil. The people were not monsters. But I felt myself moving farther away from the version of myself who had once stayed awake thinking about equations, stories, civilizations, and the beauty of ideas. I was building things, yes, but not always thinking. I was solving tasks, yes, but not always growing. I felt as if I had been made into a function call inside someone else’s system.

I wanted to escape back into books.

I wanted mathematics again.

I wanted research again.

I wanted the possibility that a day could be shaped by a theorem, a model, a strange dataset, a scientific question, or a conversation that began with one idea and ended somewhere completely unexpected.

So I went to Ireland.

At that time, Ireland was not merely a destination. It was an imagined island. I had long been fascinated by the Emerald Isle: its cliffs, old stones, rain, saints, rebels, poets, myths, fairies, leprechauns, and melancholy green light. Ireland felt like a place where history had not died but remained half-visible in the mist.

I did not go there because I thought Europe would make me superior.

I went because I needed air.

I went because I wanted to become a student again.

I went because I was tired of surviving in a form that did not feel like my own.

## Chapter III — Cork, Companionship, and the Emerald Isle

![The three musketeers — a small household republic of jokes, food, games, mathematics, and survival.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/02-three-musketeers.jpg)

*The three musketeers: the kind of friendship that makes a foreign country less foreign.*

In Ireland, I found something I had been missing for years: companionship without performance.

I met two friends who became part of the architecture of that period of my life. We cooked, argued, travelled, played Dungeons & Dragons, discussed mathematics, made jokes, and built a small world out of very ordinary things. There is a kind of friendship that does not announce itself as profound. It becomes profound because it is repeated: shared meals, shared buses, shared weather, shared poverty, shared laughter.

Ireland gave me those repetitions.

We travelled through the country, sometimes like students, sometimes like pilgrims, sometimes like fools. The cliffs looked as if the earth had been torn open by an ancient hand. The old stones stood in fields with a silence older than the modern state. There were ruins, castles, cold winds, coastal villages, and those grey skies that make even happiness feel historical.

Ireland is a country of ghosts, but gentle ones.

Not ghosts that terrify. Ghosts that accompany.

You walk near the sea and feel that myth has not entirely withdrawn from the world. The fae are perhaps not there; the leprechauns are perhaps only stories; but the mind becomes more willing to believe in thresholds. A bend in the road, a field after rain, a ruin under cloud — everything suggests that another order of reality might be hiding just behind the visible one.

For a young man who had always felt misplaced, that mattered.

I did not feel entirely at home in Ireland either. But in Ireland, not belonging felt poetic rather than humiliating.

## Chapter IV — Kinsale: A Small Beginning

![Kinsale: the trip where friendship, weather, sea air, and one unexpected person began changing the direction of my life.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/03-kinsale-trip.jpg)

*Kinsale: one of those trips that looks ordinary in memory until one realizes the future was quietly present inside it.*

And then I met her.

At first she was simply a student at the same university, a classmate of my friend, someone I met at a welcome party at the student accommodation. She looked tiny, beautiful, and impossibly young. I thought she must be twenty, much younger than me.

Later I discovered she was older than me.

This was the first of many ways in which she would defeat my assumptions.

We went to Kinsale together with friends. Kinsale was beautiful in the way small Irish coastal towns are beautiful: colour, wind, water, boats, food, a softness in the light, and the feeling that the world is large but survivable. At that time I did not know what would happen. Nobody does at the beginning of a life-changing chapter. The beginning is always disguised as a casual outing, a message, a shared joke, a photograph, a slightly longer conversation than expected.

Slowly we began talking on WhatsApp.

There are conversations that feel like communication, and there are conversations that feel like doors opening. Ours felt like doors.

I cannot explain it better than that.

It became magical, but not in the childish sense. It was magical because the world became warmer. Food tasted different. The future became less abstract. Even anxiety changed shape because there was now someone with whom anxiety could be shared.

For a while, life felt like a scene from The Big Bang Theory — friends, study, awkward comedy, scientific dreams, small rooms, big plans, love arriving through ordinary channels.

I remember thinking: perhaps life cannot be better than this.

Then reality answered.

## Chapter V — Cobh, Poverty, Work, and Love

![Cobh: our first date in a village not far from Cork, where the Titanic made its final stop before entering open sea.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/04-cobh-first-date.jpg)

*Cobh: our first date. She later joked that I looked fat and homeless. I prefer to say I looked historically burdened.*

Cobh is not far from Cork. It is beautiful, colourful, and marked by history. The Titanic stopped there before sailing into the open sea and into disaster. Even now, the place carries a strange doubleness: postcard charm and tragic memory.

That was where we had our first date.

I did not look like a prince. My wife now says I looked fat and homeless. She is not entirely wrong. I was tired, financially strained, and trying to hold together study, work, and survival in a foreign country.

This is the part of migration that people do not romanticize enough because it embarrasses the success story.

Behind every polished photograph, there are bills.

Behind every university logo, there is rent.

Behind every dream, there is the body, and the body must eat.

I worked everywhere. I trained myself as a line chef. I cooked teppanyaki-style food. I repaired vending machines. I cleaned floors. I moved stock. I stood for long hours. I learned the dignity and exhaustion of labour that leaves no elegant trace on a CV.

I did it because I had to survive.

I did it because I wanted to continue studying.

I did it because I wanted to support myself and the woman who had begun to stand beside me.

She did not abandon me.

This is not a small thing.

Many people love the version of you that is rising. Fewer people remain when you are uncertain, overworked, badly dressed, anxious, and afraid of failing. She stayed. Quietly, stubbornly, practically. And somewhere in that period I made a decision inside myself: I would marry her no matter what.

Not because life had become easy.

Because it had become shared.

## Chapter VI — The University Returns

![At University College Cork as a research assistant, beginning to return to the academic life I had been missing.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/05-ucc-ra.jpg)

*UCC: the place where I slowly returned from survival to research.*

Eventually, I got a research assistant position at University College Cork.

For me, that was not just employment. It was a return.

After the bruising years of industry, after the service jobs, after the strange double life of student and worker, I was again inside a university doing research. I was thinking, building, reading, testing ideas, working with data, and slowly recovering the part of myself that had nearly gone silent.

Ireland had given me hardship, but it had also given me friendship, love, intellectual shelter, and a second beginning.

So when people ask why I did not simply stay in Europe, the answer is not that Europe failed me. Europe gave me much. Ireland gave me some of the most important people and memories of my life.

I had chances to stay.

I had PhD opportunities in Finland and Denmark. The Western road remained open. It would have been understandable, even respectable, to follow it. Many people would have called it the obvious choice.

But by then, my life was no longer governed by obvious choices.

It was governed by love, research, and the possibility of becoming someone larger than the categories I had inherited.

## Chapter VII — Why China

![China travel: Sichuan and Tibetan landscapes, with the person who made China more than a destination.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/06-sichuan-tibet.jpg)

*Sichuan and Tibetan landscapes: China first became personal to me through love, travel, food, family, language, and daily life.*

Why did I choose China instead of Europe or America?

The honest answer is simple: ninety percent because of my wife.

I came because love had already moved the centre of my life eastward. I came because marriage is not an abstract romance. It is geography, paperwork, families, kitchens, trains, villages, festivals, arguments, translations, and the slow absorption of another civilization into one’s daily habits.

Through her, China stopped being an idea.

It became food on the table, relatives in the village, conversations I could only partly understand, jokes that needed explaining, streets full of characters I was learning to recognize, social customs I had to decode, and a rhythm of life that did not ask me to be European in order to be serious.

But the remaining ten percent also matters.

As a scientist, one must sometimes choose difficulty.

Shanghai Jiao Tong University is one of the great universities of the world. To work there, in physics-informed machine learning, computational fluid dynamics, scientific simulation, and AI for science, is not a retreat from ambition. It is ambition in another direction. It is a challenge worthy of a life that has already crossed too many borders to become provincial again.

I wanted to work on physics and CFD. I wanted to build models that connect mathematics, computation, engineering, and intelligence. I wanted the atmosphere of a university where science is not ornamental but urgent.

China offered that.

And China also offered discomfort, which is sometimes necessary.

I know there is skepticism toward Indians in parts of Chinese social media. I know there are stereotypes, jokes, suspicions, and inherited tensions. But real life is more complicated than the internet. In my wife’s village, I have talked with Chinese boys who were curious, imperfect, sometimes skeptical, but not unkind. They did not treat me as an enemy. They treated me as a person standing in front of them.

That distinction matters.

The internet reduces people to symbols. Daily life restores them to bodies.

Through marriage, my life has become more Chinese. Not completely, not performatively, not as a costume, but through absorption. Food, family, words, habits, movement, tenderness, inconvenience — culture enters not only through books but through repetition.

I did not abandon India.

I did not betray Europe.

I came to China because my life had become a bridge, and a bridge must stand somewhere.

## Chapter VIII — Shanghai and the Beginning of the PhD

![Beginning my PhD at Shanghai Jiao Tong University.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/07-sjtu-phd.jpg)

*Shanghai Jiao Tong University: a new academic beginning, and perhaps the most serious scientific challenge of my life so far.*

Standing at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, I felt something I had not expected.

Not triumph exactly.

Not arrival either.

More like consequence.

Everything had led here: the lonely boy with old books in India, the exhausted software engineer, the student escaping to Ireland, the friend at the kitchen table, the man repairing vending machines, the research assistant at UCC, the lover at Cobh, the husband crossing into a new civilization.

A PhD is often described as a degree. But for me, it is also a reckoning.

Can I become the scientist I imagined when I first fell in love with mathematics and physics?

Can I build something serious in AI for science?

Can I survive the pressure of one of the world’s strongest research environments without losing the strange literary, emotional, mythic, and hybrid parts of myself?

China is not easy. It should not be easy. A country this old, this large, this ambitious, this contradictory, this fast, and this historically dense cannot be reduced to a slogan. It must be lived slowly and imperfectly.

At SJTU, I feel the challenge every day.

That is good.

A scientist should not only seek comfort. Comfort is useful for rest, but not always for growth.

## Chapter IX — Marriage, Belonging, and the Mixed Self

![Got married 了.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/08-married.jpg)

*Got married 了: the sentence where English, life, and Chinese grammar meet halfway.*

And then we married.

Marriage made the whole journey less theoretical. Before marriage, China was partly a future plan, partly a research destination, partly my wife’s home. After marriage, it became family.

This is where identity becomes difficult.

Am I Indian?

Yes.

Am I European in some inner literary sense?

Perhaps, though Europe would laugh at the claim.

Am I becoming Chinese?

Not by blood, not by passport, not by pretending. But through love, language, food, obligation, and the daily education of marriage — yes, something Chinese is entering me.

Maybe identity is not a country.

Maybe it is a library with many rooms.

India is one room: ancestry, childhood, contradiction, wounds, pride, and the first language of survival.

Ireland is another: rain, friends, myth, hardship, love, Cork, Cobh, Kinsale, UCC, and the beginning of my return to research.

China is the room I am entering now: marriage, PhD, ambition, discomfort, family, future, and the possibility of building science at a scale I once only imagined.

Europe still lives in my sentences.

India still lives in my bones.

China is entering my habits.

Perhaps I am not confused because I lack identity. Perhaps I am confused because I have too much of it.

---

## Chapter X — Conversations in Shakespearean English

In China, I have also found a new kind of intellectual companionship.

I now have conversations with Danny, a fellow scientist and a Cambridge man. We speak, sometimes half-seriously and half-theatrically, in a kind of Shakespearean English. It is beautiful, absurd, unnecessary, and therefore deeply necessary.

This is the sort of thing people misunderstand about me.

They may think I am being snobbish.

But I am not trying to elevate myself above anyone. I am trying to make life bearable through style. I am trying to keep alive the music of the worlds that formed me. I am trying to live in a way that honours both science and language, both mathematics and manners, both research and the theatre of being human.

A person can love elegance without despising simplicity.

A person can admire aristocratic manners without endorsing arrogance.

A person can speak in old literary rhythms and still clean floors, cook for a living, repair machines, worry about rent, and fight for a future.

That is the truth of my life.

I am not a snob.

I am a displaced reader trying to become a scientist.

## Chapter XI — What the Future Holds

So what does the future hold?

I do not know exactly.

I know that I want to build serious science. I want to work at the boundary of AI, physics, computational fluid dynamics, simulation, pharmaceutical engineering, and scientific discovery. I want models that do not merely imitate data but understand structure, constraints, equations, and physical meaning.

I want to build bridges between traditions: Indian mathematical hunger, European literary and academic elegance, and Chinese scientific scale and discipline.

I want a life where my wife and I can build a family without abandoning ambition.

I want to become gentler, not harder.

I want to remain intellectually aristocratic in the best sense: devoted to learning, seriousness, beauty, responsibility, and self-discipline — never to contempt.

I want to prove that choosing China is not choosing less.

It is choosing differently.

It is refusing the old colonial map in which dignity flows only westward.

It is saying that the future of science is not owned by one civilization, one accent, one passport, or one historical centre.

It is saying that a boy who felt out of place in India, found friendship in Ireland, married into China, and speaks with ghosts from Wodehouse, Shakespeare, Euler, Ramanujan, Hardy, Einstein, Penrose, and Sir Humphrey Appleby can still make a coherent life.

Perhaps not a simple life.

But a coherent one.

And perhaps that is enough.

---

## Epilogue — The Bridge

When I look back now, I do not see a straight road.

I see rooms.

A room in India with books.

A room in Ireland with friends cooking and arguing about mathematics.

A room in Cork where I first spoke to the woman who would become my wife.

A kitchen where I worked until my body hurt.

A university office where research returned to me.

A Chinese village where I was foreign but not rejected.

A campus in Shanghai where I am trying again to become worthy of the questions that once saved me.

Maybe this is what the future holds: not a final identity, but a bridge strong enough to carry many identities at once.

India gave me origin.

Ireland gave me return.

China gave me future.

And love made the journey possible.
