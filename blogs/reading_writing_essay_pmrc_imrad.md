# Reading, Writing, and the Craft of the Essay

## PMRC Abstract

**Purpose:** This essay argues that reading and writing are mutually dependent acts, and that effective essay writing grows out of wide reading, disciplined thought, audience awareness, and structural control.

**Method:** The discussion combines reflective argument with principles drawn from academic communication and scientific writing, especially the need to know one’s audience, define a central message, maintain logical progression, and revise carefully. It also adapts the IMRAD and PMRC/BPMRC frameworks to essay writing.

**Results:** Good essays do not arise from vocabulary alone. They emerge from sustained reading, concentration on the topic, relevance of examples, a clear sense of form, and the ability to move from idea to argument without digression. Descriptive, argumentative, narrative, reflective, and factual essays each demand a distinct rhetorical discipline.

**Conclusion:** An attractive essay is not the product of inspiration alone. It is the result of reading, planning, clarity, relevance, structure, and revision. Scientific writing frameworks such as IMRAD, together with concise summary models such as PMRC, can strengthen even general essay writing by making thought more organized and communicable.

## Introduction

Reading and writing are not separate accomplishments; they are mutually sustaining acts of thought. A person rarely writes well without first learning how others have written, argued, narrated, described, and persuaded. Reading enlarges vocabulary, sharpens judgment, deepens historical awareness, and teaches the writer how language behaves in different hands. Writing, in turn, is the test of reading, because it requires a person not merely to receive knowledge but to shape it into meaningful form. For this reason, the good writer is almost always, first of all, a serious reader (Zinsser, 2012; Li, n.d.-b).

This relation becomes especially visible in essay writing. To write an essay well, one must usually know more than the topic immediately demands. An essayist needs familiarity with history, awareness of the present, and some sense of where current tendencies may lead. Even when the future is uncertain, good essays often contain measured anticipation, because thought is never fixed entirely in the present. Essays therefore require not only information but also interpretation. They ask the writer to assemble knowledge, reflect upon it, and present it in a compact but attractive form.

For many writers, essays can feel more difficult than novels. A novel may unfold through atmosphere, dialogue, character, and gradual expansion, but an essay must achieve concentration. It has limited space and must therefore choose its words with care. It should be simple without being flat, thoughtful without becoming obscure, and attractive without becoming artificial. The finest prose is often not the most ornamented but the most controlled. Clear writing is a sign of clear thinking, and disciplined language prevents the writer from wandering into vagueness or excess (Orwell, 1946; Zinsser, 2012).

The lecture materials on academic communication and scientific writing reinforce this point. Good writing must communicate an idea clearly and effectively; it depends on having something worth saying, thinking logically, and revising without mercy. The writer must know the audience, focus on the main message, and build a clear storyline rather than overwhelm the reader with everything he knows (Li, n.d.-a; Li, n.d.-b). Though these principles are taught in an academic context, they apply equally to the general essay.

## Method: Foundations of Good Essay Writing

### 1. Reading as preparation for writing

The first method of becoming a writer is to become a reader. Reading exposes one to styles, structures, arguments, tones, and vocabularies that cannot be invented in isolation. It also teaches proportion: what deserves expansion, what needs compression, and what must be left unsaid. As one of the lecture slides emphasizes, improvement in writing comes through reading, paying attention, imitating good models, and revising ruthlessly (Li, n.d.-b). In the same spirit, literature search in academic work involves reading the most relevant texts, the classics, the latest literature, and the reference chains around major works (Li, n.d.-a). The same intellectual habit benefits essayists. A writer who reads only narrowly writes narrowly.

### 2. Knowing the audience

A second requirement is audience awareness. The lectures stress that one should never assume the audience already cares about the subject, nor assume that background knowledge can be taken for granted. Rather, the audience should be treated as intelligent but not necessarily familiar with the jargon, assumptions, or abbreviations of the writer’s world (Li, n.d.-a). This advice is invaluable for essay writing. The essayist must not write merely for himself. He must ask: Who is reading this? What do they want to know? What do they already understand, and what must be explained? A good essay does not display knowledge to intimidate the reader; it presents knowledge in a way that invites the reader into thought.

### 3. Planning and concentration

Essay writing also requires plan and concentration. Before beginning, the writer should decide what he truly wishes to say. Without such a plan, writing becomes either repetitive or directionless. A central message gives the essay coherence, and a rough outline prevents the drift that often ruins otherwise promising writing. This is why academic communication places such value on defining the message before preparing slides or drafting a paper (Li, n.d.-a). The same discipline applies to essays.

Concentration on the subject is equally necessary. If a writer begins from one idea and strays into another without purpose, the essay becomes confused. To think of the cow and write about the rat is not originality; it is irrelevance. The example is humorous, but the principle is serious. Every paragraph should remain answerable to the subject. Digression may occasionally enrich an essay, but only when it returns with force to the main idea. Otherwise, it becomes a disorderly escape from the topic.

### 4. Simplicity, clarity, and relevance

Good prose depends on the right words, not merely on many words. Simplicity is not weakness; it is mastery. The lectures repeatedly value simple, well-crafted, general explanation over needless difficulty (Li, n.d.-a). Orwell likewise warns against ugliness and inaccuracy in language, because such habits corrupt thought itself (Orwell, 1946). Zinsser, too, treats clutter as the central disease of writing and rewriting as the cure (Zinsser, 2012). The essayist therefore must seek words that are faithful to meaning. Attractive language matters, but attraction without relevance becomes decoration. The aim is not verbal extravagance but expressive precision.

## Results: The Major Types of Essay

Different essays demand different forms of control. Though all essays require coherence, each type has its own rhetorical logic.

### Descriptive essays

A descriptive essay must create a vivid but disciplined picture. It should open in a manner that invites attention, move upward through increasingly revealing detail, and end in a satisfying way. Description must not become a heap of adjectives. Its purpose is to make the reader see, feel, or imagine the subject with increasing clarity.

### Argumentative essays

Argumentative essays demand the strongest internal consistency. The writer must decide his stance and sustain it with evidence, reasoning, and structure. To begin on one side and end carelessly on the other is not complexity unless the essay openly stages such a development. Otherwise, it becomes contradiction. Academic writing lectures emphasize logical progression, relevance, and the avoidance of unnecessary detail that weakens the main message (Li, n.d.-a). Argumentative essays depend on exactly this discipline.

### Narrative essays

Narrative essays are concerned with events, but they are not mere chronicles. A narrative must select and order experience so that an event gains shape and significance. The writer should not only tell what happened but also guide the reader toward why it mattered. Simplicity of tone often helps narrative writing, because the event itself should carry force.

### Reflective essays

Reflective essays resemble narrative essays in tone but place greater emphasis on inward meaning. They use an event, idea, or memory as a site of interpretation. Reflection demands honesty and measure. It should illuminate experience rather than drown it in sentimentality.

### Factual essays

Factual writing is perhaps the most demanding of all, because it leaves the least room for carelessness. Facts must be presented directly, chronologically or logically, and with due regard to evidence. Imagination may assist explanation, but exaggeration destroys credibility. In this kind of writing, one must not sacrifice truth for ornament. The lectures on scientific writing make the same point in another form: a method must be clear enough to reproduce, results must be accurately summarized, and discussion must interpret without distorting (Li, n.d.-b). Factual essays, too, require disciplined truthfulness.

## Discussion: PMRC, IMRAD, and the Essay Form

Although IMRAD is traditionally associated with scientific papers, its logic can greatly strengthen essay writing. In the lecture materials, IMRAD is presented as the general structure of a scientific paper: **Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion**, followed by conclusion and supporting components. Its value is practical: it helps authors organize their work, helps readers and reviewers save time, and helps editors manage clarity and economy (Li, n.d.-b). Even outside science, this structure encourages ordered thought.

In an essayistic adaptation, **Introduction** frames the topic, explains its importance, and narrows the discussion to a clear question. The lectures advise that an introduction should begin from the larger significance of the subject, identify what is known, indicate what is missing, and then show what the present work will do (Li, n.d.-b). A strong essay introduction can do precisely that, though in more literary language.

The **Method** section, in a non-technical essay, need not list instruments or datasets. Instead, it may describe the approach: historical reflection, comparative reading, philosophical argument, or rhetorical analysis. The point is to make the reader understand how the writer is proceeding.

The **Results** section of an essay corresponds to the writer’s main observations, examples, distinctions, and claims. It is where the argument becomes visible. In scientific abstracts, nearly half the space is often reserved for the most important results, showing how central this part is to communicative success (Li, n.d.-b). Essays, too, must devote most of their substance to the unfolding of insight rather than remain stuck in introduction.

The **Discussion** interprets the significance of what has been shown. According to the lecture slides, discussion should connect present findings with previous understanding, explain relationships among the parts, acknowledge implications and limitations, and suggest future application or thought (Li, n.d.-b). In a general essay, this becomes the space where examples are turned into meaning. Without discussion, a piece of writing may contain information but still fail to produce understanding.

Alongside IMRAD, a concise **PMRC** frame can serve as a powerful planning and summary tool:

- **P – Purpose:** What is the essay trying to establish?
- **M – Method:** How will the writer develop the argument?
- **R – Results:** What has the essay shown or clarified?
- **C – Conclusion:** What should the reader carry away?

The lecture on abstracts uses the fuller **BPMRC** pattern—Background, Purpose, Method, Results, Conclusion—to show how strong academic summaries are built (Li, n.d.-b). For essay writing, PMRC provides a compressed discipline, while BPMRC gives an even fuller model when a brief background is needed.

This connection between essay writing and academic structure should not be misunderstood. A good essay is not a laboratory report, nor should it sound mechanical. Yet structure does not kill style; rather, it protects style from shapelessness. George Whitesides describes a paper as an organized description of hypotheses, data, and conclusions, intended to instruct the reader (Whitesides, 2004). The same principle, in a broader sense, applies to essays: they are organized attempts to lead a reader from attention to understanding.

A final lesson from the lectures is the value of revision. Writing improves not only through inspiration but through reworking. Titles should be concise and informative, not vague or overloaded with unnecessary wording; abbreviations should be used with care; and the main idea should be easy to grasp from the beginning (Li, n.d.-b). These rules matter for essays as much as for papers. A weak title makes a strong piece easier to ignore, while a clear title prepares the reader for the journey ahead.

---

## Conclusion

From this discussion, it becomes clear that knowing *about* essay writing is not enough to produce a good essay. Writing depends on reading, attention, selection, vocabulary, structure, and revision. A writer must read good books and essays, cultivate interpretation, and learn to remain faithful to the subject. He must know what he wishes to say, know for whom he is writing, and know how to organize thought so that the whole moves with coherence.

An attractive essay is therefore not simply the expression of feeling, though feeling may animate it. It is the disciplined expression of thought. Reading supplies the matter; writing supplies the form. Planning prevents confusion; concentration prevents irrelevance; revision prevents excess. When these elements come together, the essay ceases to be a rough accumulation of ideas and becomes a shaped act of communication.

In that sense, the best lesson is simple: read widely, think clearly, write honestly, and revise without mercy. Then writing will no longer be only a personal act of expression; it will become an intellectual craft.
