> This is not a political manifesto. It is a personal chapter of a life: a story about inheritance, loneliness, books, friendship, love, migration, scholarship, marriage, and the strange privilege and burden of belonging to several civilizations at once. Life is not about rigid ideology; we are not born with a finished character, we must build it from the elements around us. Like the coral, as we grow, we absorb our experiences and turn them into a solid foundation.

*My “Trinity Gentleman” self: the version of me that people sometimes mistake for snobbery, when it is really a costume stitched from literature, loneliness, and admiration for a certain idea of gentleness.*

## Chapter I — The Western Door

Back in India, many people still carry a quiet assumption: to go to Europe or America is to have arrived and lived. The belief is not always spoken crudely. It comes dressed as parental pride, respectable advice, social calculation, and the old grammar of success. A son in America, a daughter in Europe, a cousin in London, a friend in Dublin, a classmate in California: these become invisible medals pinned to a family name. The map itself becomes a moral hierarchy, and the West becomes less a location than a certificate of worth.

I understand why this happens - Europe and America possess universities, laboratories, libraries, museums, legal traditions, and a long public history of intellectual prestige. One should not deny what is admirable. But beneath admiration there often remains a colonial aftertaste, a discipline of the imagination inherited from empire. It teaches us, subtly and sometimes cruelly, that dignity must be validated elsewhere, that intelligence becomes more believable when stamped by a Western institution, that refinement comes from imitation, and that success means increasing one’s distance from the place where one began. Colonial dogmatism survives not merely in laws or borders, but in the private theatre of aspiration. It makes people dream in someone else’s accent.

And yet I must confess that I, too, was shaped by that world and I perhaps drank from that well of remaining colonial aftertaste more so than others. My imagination had been furnished by English books, old comedies, detective stories, and the rituals of a civilization that felt both foreign and strangely intimate to me. I loved P. G. Wodehouse and the delicate absurdity of Jeeves and Wooster. I loved the performances of Stephen Fry and Hugh Laurie in the live adaptation of the show. The clerical gentleness of Father Brown, the deductive chill of Sherlock Holmes, the wickedness of Blackadder, and the political fencing of *Yes Minister*, where Paul Eddington’s Mr. Hacker and Nigel Hawthorne’s Sir Humphrey Appleby turned bureaucracy into an art of elegant evasion. These men, fictional and theatrical though they were, lived in my head like tutors in cadence, wit, restraint, and comic civilization.

Books widened the distance between me and the world immediately around me. I read the Brontë sisters and argued about Shakespeare and Marlowe with my English teacher. I moved from literature into mathematics and physics with the same hunger: Euler, Ramanujan, Hardy, Littlewood, Einstein, Schrödinger, Penrose, Thorne, Jacobi, Hawking, and others became part of my private constellation. I did not experience knowledge as a list of subjects. Literature, mathematics, physics, history, philosophy and comedy seemed to belong to a single aristocracy of the mind, a republic of serious play where a theorem and a sentence could be equally beautiful.

That mixture made me strange. I liked careful speech, polished dress, old books, long conversations, private codes of dignity, and a kind of theatrical gentleness that can easily be misunderstood. People sometimes read distance where there was shyness, pride where there was awkwardness, and snobbery where there was only the attempt to survive through class. I have called myself aristocratic, and perhaps that sounds dangerous in a democratic age. But I do not mean superiority. I mean inheritance, discipline, and an obligation to learning. I come from a family memory that speaks of lineage and refinement, even tracing itself in mythic imagination toward Buddha himself; yet to me such ancestry is not a throne from which to look down. It is a lamp one must be worthy of carrying. Men of Letters!

<p style="text-align:center;"><em>"Manners Maketh Man"</em></p>

To be an aristocratic gentleman in the best sense is not to sneer. It is to practice restraint. It is to value knowledge over noise, manners over aggression, responsibility over display, and gentleness over conquest. Manners without kindness are merely decoration. Education without humility is only a sharper weapon. If there is any nobility I believe in, it is not the nobility of blood, but the nobility of seriousness, self-command, and service to beauty and truth.

Still, the world does not always read the soul correctly. In India I often felt like an outcast, not because I hated my country, but because I did not know how to inhabit it simply. I was Indian, but not only Indian. I was drawn to Europe, but not to the European identity. I was modern, but haunted by older things. I worked with code, but wanted books. I learned to function in industry, but felt that some internal candle was being slowly starved of air. I did not know whether I was different, posh, theatrical, lonely, or merely badly translated. I only knew that I belonged somewhere, but not yet where I stood.

## Chapter II — The Industry Years and the Hunger to Escape

Before Ireland, I worked as a software engineer. From the outside, this looked like success. It had a salary, a respectable title, a future one could explain to relatives. But inside, I felt a slow breaking. The work itself was not evil, and the people around me were not monsters (Oh well some were maybe), but I felt myself becoming distant from the young man who had once stayed awake thinking about equations, stories, civilizations, and the beauty of ideas. I was useful, but not alive in the way I wanted to be alive. Life as I put it to my wife, was grey with no colours. I was solving tasks, but not always asking questions. I was writing code, but not always thinking. I felt as if I had been converted into a function call inside someone else’s machine haha.

There is a particular sadness in doing competent work that does not call forth the deepest parts of you. It is not dramatic enough to be tragedy, but it is constant enough to become erosion. Every day removes a little grain of yourself. You tell yourself to be practical, grateful, disciplined, adult. You tell yourself that many people would envy your position. Yet at night the old books return, and with them the accusation that you have abandoned your true country: not India, not Europe, not any nation, but the country of thought itself.

I wanted to escape back into books. I wanted mathematics again. I wanted research again. I wanted the possibility that a day could be shaped by a theorem, a strange dataset, a scientific question, a simulation, a model, or a conversation that began with one idea and ended somewhere wonderfully unexpected. I wanted to be a student again, not because students are innocent, but because students still have permission to be unfinished.

So I went to Ireland, the Emerald Isles. At that time, Ireland was not merely a destination. It was an imagined island, an emerald threshold. I had long been fascinated by its cliffs, rain, saints, rebels, poets, ruined stones, fairies, leprechauns, and melancholy green light. Ireland felt like a place where history had not completely died, where myth still moved under the grass like an old river. I went because I needed air. I went because life back home had made me tired in the soul. I went because I wanted, with a desperation I could barely explain, to begin again - start over!

## Chapter III — Cork, Companionship, and the Emerald Isle

![The three musketeers: David, me, and Ifham — a small household republic of jokes, food, games, mathematics, and survival.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/02-three-musketeers.jpg)

*The three musketeers: the kind of friendship that makes a foreign country less foreign.*

In Ireland, I found something I had been missing for years: companionship without performance. I met David, and Ifham, and together we created a small household republic made of jokes, food, games, mathematics, exhaustion, and survival. We cooked not as chefs of elegance, but as students trying to build warmth from cheap ingredients and shared time. David's family came to the isles long ago and were from Hungary, and Ifham was from Maldives very close to India. We played Dungeons & Dragons, and perhaps that was fitting, because each of us was, in some way, a traveller in a strange campaign: underfunded, over-imaginative, occasionally heroic, frequently ridiculous, always improvising.

There is a kind of friendship that does not announce itself as profound. It becomes profound because it is repeated. A meal cooked together, a bus taken together, an argument that returns three nights later, a mathematical idea half-explained while onions burn in the pan, a joke that becomes tradition, a journey undertaken because none of you had enough money to be sophisticated but all of you had enough curiosity to go anyway. Friendship in a foreign country is not merely affection. It is confidence. It keeps the ceiling from falling.

Ireland gave me those repetitions. We travelled through the country like students, pilgrims, and fools. The cliffs looked as if the earth had been torn open by an ancient hand. The sea did not merely shine; it brooded. The old stones stood in fields with a silence older than the modern state, and the wind moved around them like a language people had forgotten how to translate. We explored coasts, villages, ruins, and places where history seemed to have settled into the ground rather than passed away.

I had always been fascinated by the Irish imagination: the fae, the leprechauns, the enchanted thresholds, the sense that ordinary geography might suddenly reveal another world. I do not mean that I believed every story literally. Rather, Ireland made belief itself feel possible again. A bend in the road, a ruin under cloud, a field after rain, a cliff above an iron sea — each seemed to suggest that the visible world was only the first page. In Ireland, not belonging did not feel humiliating in the same way it had before. It felt poetic. I was still foreign, still uncertain, still financially afraid, but my displacement had acquired scenery, myth, and friends. Friends who I miss from time to time.

## Chapter IV — Kinsale and the Small Door Through Which Life Entered

![Kinsale: the trip where friendship, weather, sea air, and one unexpected person began changing the direction of my life.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/03-kinsale-trip.jpg)

*Kinsale: one of those trips that looks ordinary in memory until one realizes the future was quietly present inside it.*

And then I met her! At first she was simply another student, a classmate of Ifham, someone I met at a welcome party at the student accommodation. She looked tiny, beautiful, and impossibly young. I thought she must be twenty, much younger than me. Later I discovered she was older than me, which was the first of many ways she would defeat my assumptions with a calmness that made my theories look foolish.

<p style="text-align:center;"><em>"It is yuánfèn 缘分 that brought us together."</em></p>

We went to Kinsale together with friends. Kinsale was beautiful in the way small Irish coastal towns are beautiful: colour, wind, water, boats, seafood, narrow streets, and a softness in the light that makes even ordinary happiness appear carefully painted. At that time, I did not know what would happen. Nobody recognizes destiny "缘分" when it first enters the room. Destiny is rarely announced by thunder. More often it arrives as a casual outing, a photograph, a shared joke, a message that continues longer than expected.

Slowly we began talking on WhatsApp. There are conversations that merely exchange information, and there are conversations that open doors in the architecture of the self. Ours felt like doors. The world became warmer, not in a sentimental way, but in the way a cold room changes when someone lights a small lamp. Food tasted different. The future became less abstract. Even anxiety changed shape because there was now someone to whom anxiety could be confessed.

For a while, life felt like a scene from *The Big Bang Theory*: friends, study, awkward comedy, scientific dreams, small rooms, large ambitions, and love arriving through the most ordinary channels. I remember thinking that perhaps life could not become better than this. I had friendship, books, mathematics, Ireland, and the beginning of love. It seemed almost too generous.

Then reality, which is never impressed by romance for long, returned with its ledger.

## Chapter V — Cobh, Poverty, Work, and the Decision to Marry

![Cobh: our first date in a village not far from Cork, where the Titanic made its final stop before entering open sea.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/04-cobh-first-date.jpg)

*Cobh: our first date. She later joked that I looked fat and homeless. I prefer to say I looked historically burdened.*

Cobh is not far from Cork. It is colourful, beautiful, and marked by history. The Titanic stopped there before sailing into the open sea and into catastrophe. Even now, the place carries a strange doubleness: postcard charm and tragic memory, bright houses facing water that once received a ship on the edge of myth. It was there that we had our first date.

I did not look like a prince. My wife now says I looked fat and homeless. She is not entirely wrong. I was tired, financially strained, and trying to hold together study, work, hope, and survival in a foreign country. The photograph is precious to me precisely because it is not glamorous. It shows not the polished hero of a love story, but a man trying very hard not to collapse under the ordinary arithmetic of rent, food, tuition, time, and fear.

This is the part of migration that people do not romanticize enough because it embarrasses the success narrative. Behind every polished photograph there are bills. Behind every university logo there is rent. Behind every dream there is the body, and the body must eat. The social mythology says: he went abroad, therefore he rose. The lived truth is: he went abroad, and then he worked until his feet hurt, until his clothes smelled of kitchens, until sleep became a luxury, until pride had to be folded away because survival required humility.

I worked everywhere. I trained myself as a line chef. I cooked teppanyaki-style food. I repaired vending machines. I cleaned floors. I moved stock. I learned the dignity and exhaustion of labour that leaves no elegant trace on a curriculum vitae. These jobs did not fit the fantasy of the gentleman-scholar, but perhaps they made that fantasy more honest. It is easy to admire aristocratic manners when one has never scrubbed anything. It is better to learn that dignity survives grease, fatigue, and practical necessity.

I worked because I had to survive. I worked because I wanted to continue studying. I worked because I wanted to support myself and the woman who had begun to stand beside me. And she did stand beside me. She did not abandon me when I was uncertain, overworked, badly dressed, anxious, and afraid of failing. Many people love the version of you that is rising. Fewer remain when you are incomplete. Her loyalty was quiet, practical, and stubborn, which is perhaps the strongest kind.

Somewhere in those days I made a decision inside myself: I would marry her no matter what. Not because life had become easy, but because life had become shared. Love did not arrive as an escape from hardship. Love became the form in which hardship could be carried.

## Chapter VI — The University Returns

![At University College Cork as a research assistant with my good friend and colleague Rasika Edirisinghe, beginning to return to the academic life I had been missing.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/05-ucc-ra.jpg)

*UCC: the place where I slowly returned from survival to research, standing with my good friend and colleague [Rasika Edirisinghe](https://mrhendrixsl.github.io/).*

Eventually, I got a research assistant position at University College Cork, (courtesy to my Professor _Dr. Rosane_) working in the academic world again and standing beside colleagues who made research feel like a living community rather than an abstract ambition. For me, that position was not merely employment. It was a return from exile. After the bruising years of industry, after the service jobs, after the strange double life of student and worker, I was again inside a university doing research. I was reading, building, testing ideas, working with data, and slowly recovering the part of myself that had nearly gone silent.

There is a particular tenderness in returning to what one feared had been lost. A library does not embrace you, but it can receive you. A university does not promise salvation, but it can give shape to a scattered mind. In those days at UCC, I felt that the long detour through industry and labour had not destroyed me. It had thickened me. It had made my desire for research less romantic and more durable.

Ireland had given me hardship, but it had also given me friendship, love, intellectual shelter, and a second beginning. So when people ask why I did not simply stay in Europe, the answer is not that Europe failed me. Europe gave me much. Ireland gave me some of the most important people and memories of my life. It gave me cliffs and kitchens, friends and myths, love and labour, humiliation and recovery. It gave me the feeling that I could become a scholar again without pretending that life was easy.

I had chances to stay. I had PhD opportunities in Finland and Denmark. The Western road remained open, respectable, and understandable. Many people would have called it the obvious choice. In the old colonial arithmetic, it would have looked like success continuing in the correct direction. Yet by then my life was no longer governed by obvious choices. It was governed by love, research, and the possibility of becoming someone larger than the categories I had inherited.

## Chapter VII — Why China

![China travel: Sichuan and Tibetan landscapes, with the person who made China more than a destination.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/06-sichuan-tibet.jpg)

*Sichuan and Tibetan landscapes: China first became personal to me through love, travel, food, family, language, and daily life.*

Why did I choose China instead of Europe or America? The honest answer is simple: ninety percent because of my wife. I came because love had already moved the centre of my life eastward. I came because marriage is not an abstract romance. It is commitment to self and the person you value most in this world.

Through her, China stopped being an idea. It became food on the table, relatives in the village, conversations I could only partly understand, jokes that needed explaining, streets full of characters I was learning to recognize, and a rhythm of life that did not ask me to become Western in order to be serious. China entered my life not first as geopolitics, but as family. Not as a headline, but as breakfast. Not as ideology, but as the shopkeeper giving me my first 煎饼, the village road (农村的路), the shared meal in the University Canteen, and the patient correction of a phrase from my wife.

But the remaining ten percent also matters. As a scientist, one must sometimes choose difficulty. Shanghai Jiao Tong University is one of the great universities of the world, and to work there in physics-informed machine learning, computational fluid dynamics, scientific simulation, and AI for science is not a retreat from ambition. It is ambition in another direction. It is a challenge worthy of a life that has already crossed too many borders to remain provincial in its idea of prestige.

I wanted to work on physics and CFD. I wanted to build models that connect mathematics, computation, engineering, and intelligence. I wanted to be in a place where science is urgent, large-scale, competitive, and alive. China offered that. It offered not the familiar prestige route that many Indians are trained to imagine, but a different map of seriousness. It allowed me to say, quietly but firmly, that dignity does not only flow westward.

China also offered discomfort, and discomfort is sometimes necessary. I know there is skepticism toward Indians in parts of Chinese social media. I know there are stereotypes, jokes, suspicions, and inherited tensions. But real life is more complicated than the internet. I have spoken with Chinese men around me who were curious, imperfect, sometimes skeptical, but not unkind. They did not treat me as an enemy. They treated me as a person standing in front of them, and that distinction matters. The internet reduces people to symbols. Daily life restores them to bodies. However the hate is real and innately deep.

Through marriage, my life has become more Chinese, not completely and not performatively, but through absorption. Food, family, words, habits, obligations, tenderness, inconvenience: culture enters not only through books, but through repetition. I did not abandon India. I did not betray Europe. I came to China because my life had become a bridge, and a bridge must stand somewhere.

## Chapter VIII — Shanghai and the Beginning of the PhD

![Beginning my PhD at Shanghai Jiao Tong University.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/07-sjtu-phd.jpg)

*Shanghai Jiao Tong University: a new academic beginning, and perhaps the most serious scientific challenge of my life so far.*

Standing at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, I felt something I had not expected. It was not triumph exactly, and not arrival either. It was more like consequence. Everything had led here: the lonely boy with old books in India, the exhausted software engineer, the student escaping to Ireland, the friend at the kitchen table, the man repairing vending machines, the research assistant at UCC, the lover at Cobh, the husband crossing into a new civilization.

A PhD is often described as a degree, but for me it is also a reckoning and most importantly a childhood dream. Can I become the scientist I imagined when I first fell in love with mathematics and physics? Can I build something serious in AI for science? Can I work at the boundary of AI, computational fluid dynamics, simulation, pharmaceutical engineering, and scientific discovery? Can I survive the pressure of one of the world’s strongest research environments without losing the literary, emotional, mythic, and hybrid parts of myself?

China is not easy. It should not be easy. A country this old, this large, this ambitious, this contradictory, this fast, and this historically dense cannot be reduced to a slogan. It must be lived slowly and imperfectly. At SJTU, I feel the challenge every day, and that is good. A scientist should not only seek comfort. Comfort is useful for rest, but not always for growth.

The scientific life I want is not narrow. I want models that do not merely imitate data but understand structure, constraints, equations, and physical meaning. I want to build bridges between traditions: Indian mathematical hunger, European literary and academic elegance, and Chinese scientific scale and discipline. I want to prove, through work rather than rhetoric, that choosing China is not choosing less. It is choosing differently.

## Chapter IX — Marriage, Belonging, and the Mixed Self

![Got married 了.](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/08-married.jpg)

*Got married 了: the sentence where English, life, and Chinese grammar meet halfway my daily style of converstation with my wife.*

And then we married. Marriage made the whole journey less theoretical. Before marriage, China was partly a future plan, partly a research destination, partly my wife’s home. After marriage, it became family. It became a place where my choices had consequences beyond myself. It became a shared future rather than a personal experiment.

This is where identity becomes difficult. Am I Indian? Yes. Am I European in some inner literary sense? Perhaps, though Europe would laugh at the claim. Am I becoming Chinese? Not by blood, not by passport, not by pretending, but through love, language, food, obligation, and the daily education of marriage. Something Chinese is entering me, just as something Indian remains unremovable, and something European continues to live in my sentences.

Maybe identity is not a country. Maybe it is a library with many rooms. India is one room: ancestry, childhood, contradiction, wounds, pride, and the first language of survival. Ireland is another: rain, friends, myth, hardship, love, Cork, Cobh, Kinsale, UCC, and the beginning of my return to research. China is the room I am entering now: marriage, PhD, ambition, discomfort, family, future, and the possibility of building science at a scale I once only imagined.

I do not know whether I am confused because I lack identity or because I have too much of it. Perhaps the self is not meant to be purified into one flag, one accent, one story. Perhaps the self is sometimes a palimpsest, written over and over by travel, affection, humiliation, labour, books, and the stubborn fact that one can love more than one world without belonging completely to any of them.

## Chapter X — The Shakespearean Weather of the Mind

In China, I have also found a new kind of intellectual companionship. I now have conversations with Danny, a fellow scientist and a Cambridge man. We speak, sometimes half-seriously and half-theatrically, in a kind of Shakespearean English. It is beautiful, absurd, unnecessary, and therefore deeply necessary. Such conversations remind me that language is not merely a tool for exchanging information; it is also a stage on which the soul rehearses its possible forms.

This is the sort of thing people misunderstand about me. They may think I am being snobbish when I enjoy such elegance, but I am not trying to elevate myself above anyone. I am trying to make life interesting through style. I am trying to keep alive the music of the worlds that formed me. I am trying to live in a way that honours both science and language, both mathematics and manners, both research and as the great bard put it _"theatre of being human"_.

A person can love elegance without despising simplicity. A person can admire aristocratic manners without endorsing arrogance. A person can speak in old literary rhythms and still clean floors, cook for a living, repair machines, worry about rent, and fight for a future. That is the truth of my life. I am not a snob. I am a displaced reader trying to become a scientist.

## Chapter XI — A Life Against the Old Map

The old colonial map says that legitimacy travels in one direction. It says that the world has centres and margins, that ambition should migrate toward the approved capitals, that success requires the blessing of particular geographies. My life has not escaped that map completely; no life born into history escapes history untouched. But I have begun to draw over it.

India gave me origin, ancestry, contradiction, and hunger. Ireland gave me friendship, myth, hardship, love, and the path back to research. China has given me marriage, challenge, family, scientific ambition, and a future that does not apologize for being eastward. Europe still lives in my sentences. India still lives in my bones. China is entering my habits. My wife stands at the centre of this transformation, not as a symbol, but as the person through whom the abstract became daily and the foreign became intimate.

Perhaps the future will not give me a simple identity. Perhaps it will give me something better: a coherent one. I want to build serious science. I want to work at the boundary of AI, physics, computational fluid dynamics, simulation, and engineering. I want a life where my wife and I can build a family without abandoning ambition. I want to become gentler, not harder. I want to remain aristocratic only in the noblest sense: devoted to learning, beauty, responsibility, and self-discipline, never to contempt.

When I look back now, I do not see a straight road. I see rooms. A room in India with books. A room in Ireland with friends cooking and arguing about mathematics. A room in Cork where I first spoke to the woman who would become my wife. A kitchen where I worked until my body hurt. A university office where research returned to me. A Chinese village where I was foreign but not rejected. A campus in Shanghai where I am trying again to become worthy of the questions that once saved me.

Maybe this is what the future holds: not a final identity, but a bridge strong enough to carry many identities at once. India gave me origin. Ireland gave me return. China gave me future. Love made the journey possible.

## Epilogue — A Sonnet of Three Shores

From Indra’s cloud to Lugh’s green spear I came,  
Through Buddha’s hush and Saraswati’s stream;  
The fae lit lamps and whispered me by name,  
While dragon winds awoke my eastern dream.  
I crossed where Titan waves remember grief,  
And Cobh still keeps the sea’s last silver sigh;  
There love, like Chang’e’s moon on mortal leaf,  
Made ruined roads grow bright beneath the sky.  
No single throne can house my mingled soul,  
Nor one old map command my blood to stay;  
I am the bridge where many rivers roll,  
India’s dawn, Ireland’s mist, and China’s day.  
So let love crown what wandering began:  
Three worlds made one within one mortal man.

## 第一章 — 西方之门

在印度，许多人心里仍然保留着一种安静却顽固的假设：去了欧洲或美国，仿佛人生才算真正抵达。这个观念并不总是粗暴地说出口。它常常披着父母的骄傲、体面的建议、社会的计算，以及旧式成功叙事的外衣。一个儿子在美国，一个女儿在欧洲，一个表亲在伦敦，一个朋友在都柏林，一个同学在加州——这些名字和地点会变成无形的勋章，被别在家族的名声上。地图本身仿佛成为一种道德等级，而“西方”也不再只是地理位置，而像是一张证明你有价值的证书。

我理解这种心理为什么存在。欧洲和美国确实拥有大学、实验室、图书馆、博物馆、法治传统，以及漫长而公开的知识声望。值得敬佩的东西，我们不应该否认。可是，在这种敬佩之下，常常仍然残留着一种殖民的余味，一种由帝国遗留下来的想象力纪律。它悄悄地、有时甚至残忍地教导我们：尊严必须在别处得到认证，智慧要被西方机构盖章才更可信，优雅来自模仿，成功意味着离出发的地方越来越远。殖民式的教条并不只存活在法律或边界里，它也存活在我们内心的欲望剧场里。它让人用别人的口音做梦。

然而，我也必须承认，我自己同样被那个世界塑造过；甚至可以说，我比许多人更深地饮过那口仍带殖民余味的井。我的想象力被英语书籍、旧式喜剧、侦探故事，以及某种既陌生又亲密的文明仪式装点起来。我喜欢 P. G. Wodehouse，喜欢 Jeeves and Wooster 那种精巧而荒诞的幽默。我喜欢 Stephen Fry 和 Hugh Laurie 的表演。Father Brown 的牧师式温柔，Sherlock Holmes 的冷静推理，Blackadder 的尖刻机智，以及 *Yes Minister* 里 Paul Eddington 饰演的 Mr. Hacker 与 Nigel Hawthorne 饰演的 Sir Humphrey Appleby 把官僚主义变成优雅回避艺术的方式，都深深吸引我。那些人虽然是虚构的、舞台化的，却像住在我脑海里的老师，教我语调、机智、克制，以及文明荒谬中的喜剧感。

书本把我和周围的世界拉开了距离。我读勃朗特姐妹，也曾和英语老师争论 Shakespeare 与 Marlowe。我从文学走向数学和物理时，带着同样的饥饿感：Euler、Ramanujan、Hardy、Littlewood、Einstein、Schrödinger、Penrose、Thorne、Jacobi、Hawking，以及许多其他名字，构成了我内心的星座。我从不把知识看作一张学科清单。文学、数学、物理、历史、哲学和喜剧，在我看来属于同一个精神贵族阶层：一个严肃游戏的共和国，在那里，一个定理和一句句子可以同样美丽。

这种混合使我显得奇怪。我喜欢谨慎的措辞、整洁的衣着、旧书、长谈、私人的尊严准则，以及一种容易被误解的戏剧化温柔。人们有时把羞怯读成距离，把笨拙读成傲慢，把试图凭借风度生存的努力读成势利。我曾说自己有一种 aristocratic 的气质，在民主时代这或许听起来危险。但我说的并不是优越感，而是传承、纪律，以及对学习的责任。我来自一种关于血脉与修养的家族记忆；在我的私人神话里，它甚至把自身追溯到佛陀那里。然而对我来说，这样的祖先叙事不是一张可以俯视他人的王座，而是一盏必须配得上去提起的灯。Men of Letters！

<p style="text-align:center;"><em>“Manners Maketh Man.” / 礼仪成就人。</em></p>

在最好的意义上，做一个有贵族精神的绅士，并不是冷笑、轻视或居高临下。它意味着克制。意味着重视知识而非噪音，重视礼貌而非攻击，重视责任而非炫耀，重视温柔而非征服。没有善意的礼仪只是装饰；没有谦卑的教育只是一把更锋利的武器。如果我相信任何一种高贵，那绝不是血统的高贵，而是认真、自持、服务于美与真理的高贵。

可是，世界并不总能正确阅读一个人的灵魂。在印度，我常常觉得自己像一个局外人。不是因为我憎恨我的国家，而是因为我不知道如何简单地生活在其中。我是印度人，但又不只是印度人。我被欧洲吸引，却并不真正拥有欧洲身份。我是现代的，却被更古老的东西缠绕。我写代码，却想要书本。我学会了在工业界运转，却感觉心里有一支蜡烛正在被慢慢夺走空气。我不知道自己究竟是不同、是 posh、是戏剧化、是孤独，还是只是被翻译得不好。我只知道，我属于某个地方，但那个地方还不是我站着的地方。

## 第二章 — 工业岁月与逃离的渴望

去爱尔兰之前，我是一名软件工程师。从外面看，这像是一种成功：有薪水，有体面的头衔，有一个可以向亲戚解释的未来。可是在里面，我感到自己正在缓慢地断裂。工作本身并不邪恶，身边的人也不全是怪物——好吧，也许有些确实是——但我感觉自己正在远离那个曾经为方程、故事、文明和思想之美而熬夜的年轻人。我有用，但不是我想要的那种活着。我曾对妻子说，那时的生活是灰色的，没有颜色。我在解决任务，却并不总是在提出问题；我在写代码，却并不总是在思考。我仿佛被转换成了别人机器里的一个函数调用，哈哈。

做一份自己能够胜任，却无法召唤出内心最深部分的工作，是一种特殊的悲伤。它不够戏剧化，不能称为悲剧；却足够持续，最终变成侵蚀。每一天都从你身上带走一点细小的颗粒。你告诉自己要实际、感恩、守纪律、像个成年人。你告诉自己，许多人会羡慕你的位置。可是到了夜里，旧书又回来，随之而来的还有一种责问：你是否背叛了自己真正的国度？那个国度不是印度，不是欧洲，也不是任何国家，而是思想本身的国度。

我想逃回书本里。我想重新拥有数学。我想重新拥有研究。我想要一种可能性：一天可以被一个定理、一个奇怪的数据集、一个科学问题、一次模拟、一个模型，或者一场从一个想法开始、最终抵达意想不到之处的谈话所塑造。我想重新成为学生，不是因为学生天真，而是因为学生仍然被允许是不完成的。

于是我去了爱尔兰，去了那片 Emerald Isles。那时，爱尔兰对我来说不仅是一个目的地。它更像一个想象中的岛屿，一个翡翠色的门槛。我很早就迷恋它的悬崖、雨、圣徒、反叛者、诗人、废墟石阵、仙灵、leprechauns，以及那种忧郁的绿色光线。爱尔兰像一个历史没有完全死去的地方，神话仿佛仍像古老的河流，在草地下移动。我去那里，是因为我需要空气。是因为故乡的生活让我灵魂疲惫。也是因为我带着一种几乎无法解释的迫切，想要重新开始——start over！

## 第三章 — Cork、友谊与翡翠岛

![三个火枪手：David、我和 Ifham——一个由玩笑、食物、游戏、数学和生存组成的小小家庭共和国。](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/02-three-musketeers.jpg)

*三个火枪手：有一种友谊，会让异国不再那么像异国。*

在爱尔兰，我找到了多年缺失的东西：不需要表演的陪伴。我遇见了 David 和 Ifham，我们一起建立了一个由玩笑、食物、游戏、数学、疲惫和生存组成的小小家庭共和国。我们做饭，并不是以优雅厨师的姿态，而是以学生的方式，用便宜的食材和共同度过的时间，努力造出一点温暖。David 的家族很久以前来到这些岛屿，源自匈牙利；Ifham 来自马尔代夫，离印度很近。我们玩 Dungeons & Dragons，这也许很合适，因为我们每个人都像在一场奇异的战役中旅行：经费不足、想象过剩、偶尔英勇、经常荒唐，但总是在即兴求生。

有一种友谊并不会一开始就宣布自己深刻。它之所以深刻，是因为它不断重复。一起做的一顿饭，一起坐的一班车，三天后又被重新提起的一场争论，洋葱在锅里快焦了时还在半解释的数学想法，一个逐渐变成传统的笑话，一段不是因为有钱去精致旅行、而是因为好奇心足够强才出发的旅程。在异国的友谊不只是 affection，它也是 confidence。它让天花板不至于塌下来。

爱尔兰给了我这些重复。我们像学生、朝圣者和傻瓜一样穿越这个国家。悬崖像是大地被某只古老的手撕开。海不只是闪光；它在沉思。古老的石头立在田野里，带着比现代国家更古老的沉默，而风绕着它们吹过，像一种人们已经忘记如何翻译的语言。我们探索海岸、村庄、废墟，以及那些历史似乎沉入土地而非消逝的地方。

我一直迷恋爱尔兰的想象：fae、leprechauns、被施了魔法的门槛，以及那种普通地理突然可能显露另一个世界的感觉。我并不是说自己真的逐字相信每一个传说。更准确地说，爱尔兰让我重新觉得“相信”本身变得可能。一条转弯的路，一片云下的废墟，一场雨后的田野，一座面对铁色大海的悬崖——它们都仿佛在暗示，可见世界只是第一页。在爱尔兰，不属于某个地方不再像以前那样羞辱人。它变得有诗意。我依然是外国人，依然不确定，依然害怕钱不够，但我的漂泊有了风景、神话和朋友。有时我真的很想念他们。

## 第四章 — Kinsale，与生命进入的小门

![Kinsale：那次旅行里有友谊、天气、海风，也有一个后来改变我人生方向的人。](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/03-kinsale-trip.jpg)

*Kinsale：有些旅行在记忆里看似普通，直到后来才发现，未来早已悄悄站在其中。*

然后，我遇见了她。起初，她只是另一个学生，是 Ifham 的同学，是我在学生公寓 welcome party 上遇到的人。她看起来很小，很漂亮，也年轻得不可思议。我以为她一定只有二十岁，比我小很多。后来我才发现，她比我大。这是她第一次用一种冷静的方式击败我的假设，并让我的理论显得很愚蠢。

<p style="text-align:center;"><em>“It is yuánfèn 缘分 that brought us together.” / 是缘分把我们带到了一起。</em></p>

我们和朋友一起去了 Kinsale。Kinsale 的美，是爱尔兰海边小镇特有的美：颜色、风、水、船、海鲜、狭窄街道，以及一种让普通幸福都像被细心画出来的柔光。那时，我不知道后来会发生什么。没有人能在命运“缘分”第一次走进房间时就认出它。命运很少伴随雷声登场。它更多时候只是一次随意的出行，一张照片，一个共同的笑话，或者一条比预想中持续得更久的信息。

慢慢地，我们开始在 WhatsApp 上聊天。有些对话只是交换信息；有些对话却像是在自我的建筑里打开一扇门。我们的对话就像门。世界变暖了，不是廉价的感伤，而像一间冷房里有人点起了一盏小灯。食物尝起来不一样了。未来不再那么抽象。连焦虑也改变了形状，因为现在有一个人，可以听我承认自己的焦虑。

有一段时间，生活像 *The Big Bang Theory* 里的场景：朋友、学习、笨拙的喜剧、科学梦想、小房间、大抱负，以及通过最普通渠道到来的爱情。我记得自己曾想，也许生活不会比这更好了。我有友谊、书本、数学、爱尔兰，还有爱情的开始。这一切慷慨得几乎不真实。

然后，现实带着它的账本回来了。现实从来不会被浪漫打动太久。

## 第五章 — Cobh、贫穷、工作与结婚的决定

![Cobh：我们第一次约会的地方，一个离 Cork 不远的小镇；泰坦尼克号在进入大海并沉没之前，最后停靠过这里。](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/04-cobh-first-date.jpg)

*Cobh：我们的第一次约会。她后来开玩笑说我那时又胖又像无家可归。我更愿意说，我看起来像背负了历史。*

Cobh 离 Cork 不远。它色彩明亮，很美，也被历史标记着。泰坦尼克号在那里停靠之后，驶向开阔的大海，最终走向灾难。即使现在，那个地方仍有一种奇怪的双重性：一边是明信片般的可爱，一边是悲剧记忆；明亮的房子面对着水，而那片水曾经送走一艘几乎进入神话的船。我们第一次约会，就在那里。

我看起来并不像王子。我的妻子现在说我那时又胖又像无家可归。她也不完全错。我疲惫、拮据，试图在异国把学习、工作、希望和生存勉强握在一起。那张照片对我来说很珍贵，正因为它并不光鲜。它展示的不是爱情故事里被打磨过的英雄，而是一个人努力不被房租、食物、学费、时间和恐惧这些普通算术压垮。

这正是迁徙故事里人们不太愿意浪漫化的部分，因为它会让成功叙事尴尬。每一张漂亮照片背后都有账单。每一个大学 logo 背后都有房租。每一个梦想背后都有身体，而身体必须吃饭。社会神话说：他出国了，所以他上升了。生活真相是：他出国了，然后工作到双脚疼痛，衣服带着厨房味，睡眠变成奢侈，骄傲不得不被折叠收起，因为生存需要谦卑。

我什么工作都做。我把自己训练成 line chef。我做 teppanyaki-style food。我修自动售货机。我擦地。我搬货。我学会了那种不会在简历上留下优雅痕迹的劳动尊严与疲惫。这些工作并不符合“绅士学者”的幻想，但也许正是它们让那个幻想变得更诚实。一个人从未刷洗过任何东西时，很容易欣赏贵族礼仪；更好的学习是明白，尊严可以在油污、疲惫和现实必要性中继续存在。

我工作，是因为我必须活下去。我工作，是因为我想继续学习。我工作，也是因为我想支持自己，以及那个已经开始站在我身边的女人。她确实站在我身边。当我不确定、过劳、穿得不好、焦虑、害怕失败时，她没有离开我。很多人爱的是你正在上升的版本；更少的人会在你尚未完成时留下。她的忠诚是安静的、实际的、固执的，而这也许是最强的一种。

就在那些日子里，我在心里做了一个决定：无论如何，我都要娶她。不是因为生活变容易了，而是因为生活变成了共同承担。爱情并不是作为逃离困难的出口到来；爱情变成了承载困难的形式。

## 第六章 — 大学归来

![在 University College Cork 做研究助理，与我的好朋友和同事 Rasika Edirisinghe 一起，开始回到我一直怀念的学术生活。](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/05-ucc-ra.jpg)

*UCC：我从生存慢慢回到研究的地方，和我的好朋友、同事 [Rasika Edirisinghe](https://mrhendrixsl.github.io/) 站在一起。*

后来，我在 University College Cork 得到了一个研究助理职位——也要感谢我的教授 _Dr. Rosane_。我重新在学术世界里工作，也和那些让研究像一个真实共同体而不是抽象抱负的同事站在一起。对我来说，那个职位不仅仅是一份工作。它更像是从流亡中归来。经历过工业界的挫伤，经历过服务业的工作，经历过学生与劳动者之间那种奇怪的双重生活之后，我又一次进入大学做研究。我阅读、搭建、测试想法、处理数据，并慢慢找回那个几乎沉默下去的自己。

回到一个你曾害怕已经失去的东西身边，会有一种特别的温柔。图书馆不会拥抱你，但它可以接纳你。大学不会许诺救赎，但它可以给散乱的心智以形状。在 UCC 的那些日子里，我感觉工业与劳动那段漫长绕路并没有摧毁我。它让我变厚了。它让我的研究欲望不再只是浪漫，而是更耐久。

爱尔兰给了我艰难，也给了我友谊、爱情、知识庇护和第二次开始。所以当人们问我为什么没有干脆留在欧洲时，答案并不是“欧洲让我失望”。欧洲给了我很多。爱尔兰给了我生命中最重要的一些人和记忆。它给了我悬崖和厨房，朋友和神话，爱情和劳动，羞辱和恢复。它让我感觉，自己可以再次成为学者，而不必假装生活很容易。

我曾有机会留下。我拿到过芬兰和丹麦的 PhD 机会。那条西方道路仍然敞开、体面、也可以理解。很多人会说那是显而易见的选择。在旧的殖民算术里，那会看起来像是成功继续朝“正确方向”前进。然而到那时，我的生活已经不再由显而易见的选择主宰。它由爱情、研究，以及成为一个比我继承的类别更大的人这种可能性所主宰。

## 第七章 — 为什么是中国

![中国旅行：四川与藏地风景，和那个让中国不再只是一个目的地的人在一起。](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/06-sichuan-tibet.jpg)

*四川和藏地风景：中国最初是通过爱情、旅行、食物、家庭、语言和日常生活，变得与我有关。*

为什么我选择中国，而不是欧洲或美国？最诚实的答案很简单：百分之九十是因为我的妻子。我来，是因为爱情已经把我生命的中心向东方移动。我来，是因为婚姻不是抽象的浪漫，而是对自己、也对这个世界上你最珍视的人作出的承诺。

通过她，中国不再只是一个概念。它变成餐桌上的食物，村里的亲戚，我只能部分听懂的对话，需要解释的笑话，街上那些我正在学习辨认的汉字，以及一种不要求我必须变得“西方”才算严肃的生活节奏。中国进入我的生命，最初不是作为地缘政治，而是作为家庭；不是作为新闻标题，而是作为早餐；不是作为意识形态，而是作为店主递给我的第一个煎饼，作为农村的路，作为大学食堂里的一顿饭，作为妻子耐心纠正我的一个词。

但剩下的百分之十同样重要。作为科学家，一个人有时必须选择困难。上海交通大学是世界上优秀的大学之一，而在那里做 physics-informed machine learning、computational fluid dynamics、scientific simulation 和 AI for science，并不是从雄心中撤退。那是另一种方向的雄心。对于一个已经跨越太多边界的人来说，这是一个足够配得上他生命的挑战，也让他不必再把声望想象得如此狭窄。

我想做物理和 CFD。我想建立连接数学、计算、工程和智能的模型。我想待在一个科学紧迫、规模巨大、竞争激烈且充满生命力的地方。中国给了我这一点。它给我的不是许多印度人被训练去想象的那条熟悉的声望路线，而是一张不同的严肃地图。它让我可以安静却坚定地说：尊严并不只向西流动。

中国也给了我不适，而不适有时是必要的。我知道，在中国社交媒体的一些地方，对印度人存在怀疑。我知道有刻板印象、玩笑、猜疑和历史遗留下来的紧张。但真实生活比互联网复杂。我和身边一些中国男人聊过，他们好奇、不完美，有时也怀疑，但并不冷酷。他们没有把我当作敌人，而是把我当作站在他们面前的一个人。这种区别很重要。互联网把人压缩成符号；日常生活把人还原成身体。当然，我也不想粉饰：这种敌意确实存在，有时甚至根深蒂固。

通过婚姻，我的生活变得更中国。不是完全地，也不是表演式地，而是通过吸收。食物、家庭、词语、习惯、义务、温柔、不便——文化不仅通过书本进入人，也通过重复进入人。我没有抛弃印度。我没有背叛欧洲。我来到中国，是因为我的生命已经变成一座桥，而桥总要站在某个地方。

## 第八章 — 上海与博士的开始

![在上海交通大学开始我的博士阶段。](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/07-sjtu-phd.jpg)

*上海交通大学：一个新的学术开始，也许也是我至今为止最严肃的科学挑战。*

站在上海交通大学时，我感到了一种自己没有预料到的东西。那并不完全是胜利，也不完全是抵达。更像是结果。所有事情都通向这里：那个在印度与旧书为伴的孤独男孩，那个疲惫的软件工程师，那个逃向爱尔兰的学生，那个在厨房桌边与朋友聊天的人，那个修自动售货机的人，那个 UCC 的研究助理，那个在 Cobh 恋爱的人，那个作为丈夫跨入另一种文明的人。

博士常常被描述成一个学位，但对我来说，它也是一次清算；更重要的是，它是一个童年梦想。我能否成为当初爱上数学与物理时想象中的科学家？我能否在 AI for science 里做出严肃的东西？我能否在 AI、computational fluid dynamics、simulation、pharmaceutical engineering 和 scientific discovery 的交界处工作？我能否在世界上最强的研究环境之一的压力中生存下来，同时不失去那些文学的、情感的、神话的、混合的自我部分？

中国并不容易。它也不应该容易。一个如此古老、如此庞大、如此有雄心、如此矛盾、如此快速、又如此历史密集的国家，不能被简化成一句口号。它必须被缓慢地、不完美地生活。在 SJTU，我每天都能感到挑战，而这很好。科学家不应该只寻找舒适。舒适适合休息，但不总适合成长。

我想要的科学生活并不狭窄。我想建立的模型，不只是模仿数据，而是理解结构、约束、方程和物理意义。我想在传统之间架桥：印度的数学饥饿，欧洲的文学与学术优雅，中国的科学规模与纪律。我想通过工作而不是口号证明，选择中国并不是选择更少，而是选择不同。

## 第九章 — 婚姻、归属与混合的自我

![Got married 了。](/assets/img/blogs/why-china/08-married.jpg)

*Got married 了：这句话里，英语、生活和中文语法在半路相遇，也像我和妻子日常聊天的方式。*

然后，我们结婚了。婚姻让整个旅程不再只是理论。在结婚之前，中国一部分是未来计划，一部分是研究目的地，一部分是我妻子的家。结婚之后，它变成了家庭。它变成一个我的选择会影响到不止我自己的地方。它不再是个人实验，而是共同的未来。

也正是在这里，身份变得困难。我是印度人吗？是。我在某种内在的文学意义上是欧洲人吗？也许吧，尽管欧洲听了大概会笑。我正在变成中国人吗？不是通过血统，不是通过护照，也不是通过假装，而是通过爱、语言、食物、义务，以及婚姻每天给我的教育。某种中国的东西正在进入我，就像某种印度的东西永远无法从我身上移除，而某种欧洲的东西仍然住在我的句子里。

也许身份不是一个国家。也许它是一座有许多房间的图书馆。印度是其中一个房间：祖先、童年、矛盾、伤口、骄傲，以及生存的第一种语言。爱尔兰是另一个房间：雨、朋友、神话、艰难、爱情、Cork、Cobh、Kinsale、UCC，以及我回到研究的开始。中国是我现在正在进入的房间：婚姻、博士、雄心、不适、家庭、未来，以及在一个我曾经只敢想象的尺度上做科学的可能。

我不知道自己究竟是因为缺少身份而困惑，还是因为拥有太多身份而困惑。也许自我本来就不该被净化成一面旗帜、一种口音、一个故事。也许自我有时是一张被反复书写的羊皮纸，被旅行、爱、羞辱、劳动、书本，以及一个顽固的事实一遍遍写过：一个人可以爱不止一个世界，却不完全属于任何一个世界。

## 第十章 — 心中的莎士比亚天气

在中国，我也找到了一种新的智识陪伴。我现在会和 Danny 聊天，他是我的科学家朋友，也是一位 Cambridge man。我们有时半认真、半戏剧化地用一种 Shakespearean English 说话。它美丽、荒诞、没有必要，却又因此显得非常必要。这样的对话提醒我：语言不只是交换信息的工具，它也是灵魂排练自己可能形态的舞台。

这正是人们容易误解我的地方。他们可能以为我享受这种优雅就是势利，但我并不是想把自己抬到别人之上。我只是试图用风格让生活变得有趣。我试图保留那些塑造过我的世界的音乐。我试图以一种同时尊重科学与语言、数学与礼仪、研究与那位伟大诗人所谓“人之为人的剧场”的方式生活。

一个人可以热爱优雅，而不轻视朴素。一个人可以欣赏贵族式的礼仪，而不支持傲慢。一个人可以用古老的文学节奏说话，同时仍然擦过地、做过饭、修过机器、担心过房租，并为未来搏斗。这就是我生命的真相。我不是一个 snob。我只是一个流离的读者，正在努力成为科学家。

## 第十一章 — 逆着旧地图生活

旧的殖民地图说，合法性只朝一个方向流动。它说世界有中心和边缘，雄心应该迁向被认可的首都，成功需要某些地理位置的祝福。我的生命并没有完全逃离这张地图；没有任何生在历史中的生命可以毫发无损地逃离历史。但我已经开始在它上面重新作画。

印度给了我起源、祖先、矛盾和饥饿。爱尔兰给了我友谊、神话、艰难、爱情，以及回到研究的道路。中国给了我婚姻、挑战、家庭、科学雄心，以及一个不必为自己向东而道歉的未来。欧洲仍然住在我的句子里。印度仍然住在我的骨头里。中国正在进入我的习惯。我的妻子站在这一切转变的中心，不是作为一个象征，而是作为那个让抽象变成日常、让陌生变得亲密的人。

也许未来不会给我一个简单的身份。也许它会给我更好的东西：一个连贯的身份。我想做严肃的科学。我想在 AI、physics、computational fluid dynamics、simulation 和 engineering 的边界上工作。我想要一种生活：我和妻子能够建立家庭，同时不放弃雄心。我想变得更温柔，而不是更坚硬。我想只在最高贵的意义上保持 aristocratic：献身于学习、美、责任和自律，而永远不走向轻蔑。

如今回头看，我看见的不是一条直路。我看见的是房间。一个在印度、装满书的房间。一个在爱尔兰、朋友们一边做饭一边争论数学的房间。一个在 Cork、我第一次和后来成为妻子的女人说话的房间。一个厨房，我在那里工作到身体疼痛。一个大学办公室，研究在那里回到我身边。一个中国村庄，我在那里是外国人，却没有被拒绝。一个上海校园，我在那里又一次努力让自己配得上那些曾经拯救过我的问题。

也许未来给予我的，不是最终的身份，而是一座足够坚固、可以同时承载多重身份的桥。印度给了我起源。爱尔兰给了我归来。中国给了我未来。爱情让这段旅程成为可能。

## 尾声 — 三岸十四行

我自因陀罗云下向前行，  
穿过佛陀静默与辩才天之泉；  
妖精为我点灯，轻呼我名，  
东方龙风唤醒沉睡的梦田。  
我越过泰坦浪仍记得的悲伤，  
Cobh 还保存着海最后的银叹；  
在那里，爱如嫦娥月落凡叶上，  
让荒路在天光下重新灿烂。  
没有一座王座能容纳混合的魂，  
也没有旧地图能命令血脉停留；  
我是桥，让许多河在身上奔涌，  
印度的黎明、爱尔兰的雾、中国的昼。  
愿爱为漂泊开始之事加冕：  
三重世界，合成一介凡人。
